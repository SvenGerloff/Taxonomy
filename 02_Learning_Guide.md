Module 4 — The European Union Taxonomy: A Framework for Sustainability
Target: EQF Level 5 (management/supervision). Audience: practical aquaculture and fishing professionals (Lars, Maria, Ibrahim) with deep field experience but no prior regulatory knowledge. This Learning Guide translates the EU Taxonomy into day-to-day choices, measurements and supervisory responsibilities. It focuses on applied actions, clear KPIs, simple monitoring templates and decision-aid tools that a skipper, farm manager or technical supervisor can use to demonstrate sustainability to buyers, banks and regulators.

Important note before you start: this guide is instructional and practical — not legal advice. The EU Taxonomy is a legal instrument with delegated acts and technical screening criteria that change over time. Use this guide to understand concepts, prepare operations and collect evidence. For formal certification, reporting or legal questions, consult your corporate advisor, certifier or legal counsel and the official EU Taxonomy documents.

PART A — Topics (each topic below contains multiple short practical sections with examples for the three personas)

1) What is the EU Taxonomy? (history, purpose, mechanics)
Paragraph 1 — Plain purpose and history (practical starter)
The EU Taxonomy is a classification tool the EU built to define which economic activities are considered environmentally sustainable. It was created to stop “greenwashing” — the practice of making claims of sustainability without consistent standards. For people on deck or running a farm, think of it as a common rulebook: if an activity (for example, installing solar on a hatchery, switching to closed cages, or reducing fuel use) meets the Taxonomy’s criteria, banks and buyers can more confidently call it “green” and finance it. The Taxonomy law was adopted by the EU in 2020 and has been progressively fleshed out through technical screening criteria and delegated acts. For you this means the high-level goal is stable, but the specific numbers and tests that say “yes” or “no” get updated by the EU technical experts. Treat this guide as an operational map to navigate those tests, not as the final legal text.

Paragraph 2 — Mechanics, in plain language: how it works on the ground
Mechanically, think of the Taxonomy as three steps that every activity must pass: (1) Make a substantial contribution to at least one of the six environmental objectives; (2) Do No Significant Harm (DNSH) to any of the other five objectives; and (3) Respect minimum social and governance safeguards (the “minimum safeguards”). For a fish farm, the “substantial contribution” might be a measurable reduction in greenhouse gas emissions per kg of fish produced. “DNSH” would force you to show that this reduction does not come at the expense of biodiversity — for example, by discharging untreated sludge that kills benthic life. So when lenders ask for Taxonomy alignment, they are looking for evidence that you passed all three steps and that you have the measurements and procedures to prove it.

Paragraph 3 — Technical screening criteria, NACE codes and reporting
The EU provides Technical Screening Criteria (TSC) that specify the exact thresholds and tests for each eligible economic activity. Those criteria are detailed by activity identifiers (often linked to NACE codes, the EU business classification system). For aquaculture this means certain NACE activities (e.g., freshwater fish farming, marine aquaculture or processing) will have bespoke thresholds (energy intensity, nutrient discharge limits, waste valorization targets). On the ground, managers must map their facility’s activities to these NACE codes, collect the required data (energy use, water use, nutrient loads, waste streams), and be ready to demonstrate compliance. Companies that must report under Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) or that seek green financing will use these mappings and the TSCs to report Taxonomy-aligned economic activities.

Paragraph 4 — Practical implication summary for the crew and managers
For Lars, Maria and Ibrahim this means: start simple and work in order. 1) Map your activities (which parts of your operation are covered). 2) Start collecting the measurements the Taxonomy will ask for (fuel logs, kWh used per kg produced, m3 of water recirculated, nutrient discharge measured monthly, percent of organic waste valorized). 3) Use the Taxonomy logic (substantial contribution + DNSH + minimum safeguards) as a checklist when making investments or operational changes. Keep records, because banks and buyers will want evidence. Finally, stay aware: delegated acts and technical criteria evolve, so schedule periodic reviews—every 6–12 months—to align measurements and investments with the specific thresholds that matter for financing and procurement decisions.

2) The six environmental objectives of the EU Taxonomy (deep dive — legal and practical)
Paragraph 1 — Overview of the six objectives and their practical meaning
The Taxonomy defines six environmental objectives: (1) climate change mitigation; (2) climate change adaptation; (3) sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources; (4) transition to a circular economy; (5) pollution prevention and control; (6) protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems. Legally, every activity that wants to be called taxonomy-aligned must either substantially contribute to at least one of these objectives and must not significantly harm the others (DNSH). Practically, this shifts how operations are judged: not only do you need to reduce emissions, but you must also show you aren’t harming water quality or biodiversity in doing so. For frontline operators, this means decisions like switching to a fuel-efficient engine must also be evaluated for possible side-effects, like increased risk of escaped fish if the vessel’s net handling procedures are changed.

Paragraph 2 — How the legal test looks in practice for managers
Under the EU rules, “substantial contribution” is not a vague phrase — specific technical screening criteria define what counts (for some activities, precise numbers are provided; for others, qualitative tests apply). The DNSH rules work as a checklist against the other five objectives: even if an activity reduces GHGs it must not degrade biodiversity or increase pollution beyond certain thresholds. Minimum safeguards require adherence to international standards on human rights, labour and governance. For facility managers this means every operational choice needs documented reasoning: what objective are we targeting, how do we measure results, how do we ensure no harm elsewhere, and how do we document compliance with social safeguards (e.g., worker safety or contractual terms)?

Paragraph 3 — Practical matrix approach to apply the six objectives in aquaculture
A practical way to use the six objectives is to build a matrix that maps each operation to all six objectives and tracks whether it provides a substantial contribution, risks harm, or has mitigation measures in place. For example, feed optimization might substantially contribute to mitigation (lower methane or CO2 per kg) and circular economy (less waste), while having potential DNSH risks for water quality if not paired with improved effluent treatment. This matrix is the backbone of the evidence file you’ll give buyers and banks. It forces trade-offs to be explicit and makes it visible which investments (e.g., installing a biofilter) are required to meet DNSH for multiple objectives.

Paragraph 4 — Examples tailored to each persona
For Lars (skipper): the six objectives translate into concrete deck-level actions (fuel choice and records for mitigation; route planning for adaptation; bycatch measures for biodiversity; proper waste capture for circular economy; minimizing chemical spills for pollution control; and avoiding sensitive habitats for water resource protection). For Maria (farm manager): each objective will map to investments and KPIs — e.g., meters and dashboards to track kWh/kg (mitigation), robust inlet screens and backup pumps for climate adaptation, RAS and settle tanks for water protection, on-site biogas for circular economy, dosing controls and substitution of antibiotics/chemicals for pollution prevention, and fallowing and benthic monitoring plans for biodiversity. For Ibrahim (technical lead): objectives become SOPs and checklists — daily checks on energy meters, maintenance plans for solar-battery inverters, effluent sampling steps, and incident-reporting templates. Use the six-objective matrix and keep it updated quarterly.

3) Aquaculture and its alignment with the EU Taxonomy (how a farm physically audits and aligns)
Paragraph 1 — Start with mapping and documentation
The first step to align a farm with the Taxonomy is to create a clear operations map: list every activity (hatchery, grow-out, feed processing, transport, packaging, sludge treatment) and assign the relevant NACE codes. For each activity, document the current baseline metrics: energy consumption (kWh), fuel use (liters), production volumes (kg), water intake and discharge volumes (m3), nutrient concentrations in effluent (mg/L of nitrogen and phosphorus), waste quantities (kg of sludge), and any valorization rates (percentage of waste converted to feed, compost, or biogas). This baseline is essential because the Taxonomy assesses “substantial contributions” often relative to intensity (e.g., energy per kg produced), so you need reliable historical numbers to show improvements or to meet absolute thresholds. Create a simple audit checklist and schedule a one-week intensive measurement period to capture realistic operational numbers.

Paragraph 2 — Physical audit steps and monitoring equipment recommendations
A physical audit should cover energy metering (separate meters per major energy uses: pumps, aeration, heating), water flow meters at inlets and discharge points, periodic laboratory analysis of effluent for BOD, COD, total nitrogen, total phosphorus and suspended solids, and scales for quantifying solid waste and sludge. For small farms, low-cost options like clamp-on energy meters, ultrasonic flow meters for pipes, and a contracted monthly lab test for a set of effluent parameters can be sufficient to build an evidence file. Keep a chain-of-custody log for samples and an electronic record (a simple spreadsheet or mobile app) for daily entries. All meters and samplers should be labeled and assigned to a responsible operator (for Lars this could mean a crew log; for Maria and Ibrahim, assign specific supervisors).

Paragraph 3 — Translating audit outcomes into Taxonomy criteria
Once you have baseline numbers, the next step is to map them to the Technical Screening Criteria for your NACE-coded activities. If the Taxonomy has a specific energy intensity threshold for a given activity (kWh/kg), check whether your farm meets it. If not, identify the gap and prioritize investments that reduce the intensity or otherwise make a substantial contribution to a different objective (e.g., circular economy through waste valorization). For DNSH, test whether any existing practice breaches thresholds for water pollution, biodiversity impacts or other objectives. For example, if effluent nutrient loads exceed a regional limit, you will need mitigation measures (settling tanks, biofiltration, or RAS upgrades) before claiming Taxonomy alignment.

Paragraph 4 — Practical evidence file and reporting routine
Create a modular evidence file: (A) operation mapping and NACE codes; (B) baseline datasets with signed daily logs and meter calibrations; (C) descriptions and photos of installed equipment and investment plans; (D) a DNSH risk register showing how each risk is mitigated; (E) indicators and KPIs with monthly dashboards; (F) staff training records and minimum safeguard documentation. For finance/buyer interactions, produce an executive one-page summary that states which activities you consider Taxonomy-aligned and includes the main KPIs. Schedule a quarterly internal review to update the evidence file and adapt targets to evolving Technical Screening Criteria. For smaller operations, a lightweight version with the same structure (but simpler datasets) will suffice — the principle is consistent documentation, not complexity.

4) Climate change mitigation (specific technologies: solar hybrid systems, feed optimization)
Paragraph 1 — What mitigation means in aquaculture and why it matters operationally
Climate change mitigation in the Taxonomy means reducing greenhouse gas emissions associated with producing a unit of product (e.g., kg of fish). For aquaculture that includes scope 1 (on-site fuel combustion on vessels or for backup generators), scope 2 (purchased electricity for pumps and lights), and some supply-chain elements (feed production). Practically, mitigation is measured as intensity metrics (e.g., kg CO2e per kg of fish) or by meeting specified energy efficiency thresholds. Operations must therefore focus on both reducing energy demand (efficiency) and switching energy supply to lower-carbon sources (renewables, cleaner fuels). For a skipper, this translates into better route planning, engine maintenance and switching to hybrid propulsion where possible. For a farm manager it maps to investments in solar arrays, efficient pumps and optimized feed to reduce indirect emissions.

Paragraph 2 — Solar hybrid systems and energy storage in practice
Solar hybrid systems (solar PV combined with battery storage and possibly a generator) are among the most practical mitigation technologies for land-based aquaculture. On the farm, place PV arrays on roof space, shade structures and nearby land where possible. Pair them with a battery and an inverter that can handle peak loads (a hybrid inverter). For hatcheries, sizing should be based on a load profile: measure hourly energy use for pumps, heaters, lights and aeration for a representative week. Install a simple energy management system (EMS) or a monitoring panel showing kWh produced, kWh consumed, and battery state-of-charge. Maintenance SOPs are critical: periodic cleaning of panels, inspection of DC cabling, and battery health checks. For the skipper (vessel), a small hybrid setup can mean an electric-assist for winches or a shore-power charging point for battery propulsion to reduce diesel use while in ports or for low-speed operations.

Paragraph 3 — Feed optimization as a mitigation lever
Feed is often the single largest indirect source of emissions in aquaculture. Optimizing feed means reducing feed conversion ratio (FCR), sourcing low-carbon feed ingredients, and reducing feed waste through improved feeding practices and feed formulations. Practical steps include: using pellet sizes optimized for fish size; implementing sensor-based feeding systems that detect appetite; regular feed audits to log feed delivered vs. growth achieved; and working with suppliers to procure feed with lower embedded emissions (e.g., shifting from high-trophic fishmeal to sustainably sourced plant or insect proteins where suitable). Create a simple monthly KPI: FCR and feed kg per kg biomass gain. For technical staff, SOPs should include feeder calibration, feed storage to prevent spoilage, daily feeding logs and quick checks for uneaten feed to adjust rationing.

Paragraph 4 — Quick-win operational practices and KPIs for the three personas
Quick wins for Lars: maintain engines to optimal tuning (fuel filters, appropriate propeller selection), record fuel per trip, measure catch-per-unit-effort to ensure reduced emissions per kg caught. For Maria: begin with an energy baseline and target a 10–15% reduction through LED lighting, variable-speed drive pumps, and a solar install; track kWh/kg monthly. For Ibrahim: set up daily energy checks, log battery cycles, perform weekly calibration of feeders, and create a short troubleshooting checklist for inverter and pump failures to avoid emergency generator use (which spikes emissions). All three should use simple KPIs on laminated posters or a single dashboard: fuel L/kg, kWh/kg, FCR, and % energy from renewables — these are compelling numbers clients and lenders understand.

5) Climate change adaptation (infrastructure changes: robust moorings, temperature monitoring)
Paragraph 1 — Difference between mitigation and adaptation, and why adaptation is required under Taxonomy
Climate adaptation is about increasing resilience to climate impacts — extreme weather, sea-level rise, temperature swings, and shifting disease dynamics. The Taxonomy recognizes adaptation as a distinct objective because reducing emissions (mitigation) doesn’t prevent physical risks. Legally, activities that protect or enhance resilience can be considered as contributing to adaptation. Practically for aquaculture, adaptation means investing in infrastructure and operational changes that reduce downtime, losses and escape risk when storms, heatwaves or floods occur. For a skipper that may mean stronger moorings and updated weather protocols; for a farm manager, it includes backup power, cooling systems, and site elevation planning.

Paragraph 2 — Robust moorings, cage design and site hardening
For coastal or sea-cage operations, robust mooring systems are essential. Mooring upgrades (bigger anchors, elastic bridle systems, redundancy in mooring lines) reduce the chance of pens breaking free during storms. Cage designs that reduce hydrodynamic loads (streamlined frames, flexible netting) also help. For lease-based farms, consider a documented mooring inspection schedule with clear criteria for line replacement. Maintain spare lines and a rapid-response kit on-site. For small-scale fishers, document mooring improvements and emergency recovery procedures that you can present when buyers or insurers ask how you reduced climate risk.

Paragraph 3 — Temperature monitoring, early-warning systems and infrastructure for thermal events
Temperature monitoring systems (simple data loggers or real-time sensors connected to a phone app) are essential for early warning of heat stress or cold snaps. Install multiple sensors across production units because water temperature can vary with depth and flow. For high-temperature events, have SOPs: reduce feeding, increase water exchange (where possible and permitted), deploy cooling (for land-based systems, heat exchangers or shading), or initiate helping measures such as adding oxygenation and reducing stocking density as per species guidelines. Document historical events and interventions; insurers and lenders like to see evidence of scenario planning (heatwave response plans) and regular drills.

Paragraph 4 — Power redundancy, flood-proofing and human-safety plans
Adaptation also means reliable power and flood resilience. Install redundancy: backup generators (kept serviced), battery backups sized to keep critical pumps and aeration running for a fixed outage period (e.g., 24–48 hours), and raised electrical panels above known flood levels. Physically protect essential infrastructure (elevation, bunds, flood barriers) and store critical spare parts and feed on elevated platforms. Maintain and practice crew evacuation and emergency response plans for extreme events. For supervisors (Ibrahim), run quarterly drills that include switch-over to backup power, manual feeding procedures, and emergency net repairs. Keep the results in a log to show continuous preparedness.

6) Sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources (RAS, water treatment specifics)
Paragraph 1 — The practical need for water stewardship in aquaculture
Water stewardship under the Taxonomy focuses on minimizing water withdrawal, protecting water quality and ensuring sustainable use of marine and freshwater resources. In aquaculture this covers intake water sourcing, discharge quality, and impacts on downstream users and ecosystems. Rigorously measuring inflow and outflow volumes and effluent constituents (N, P, BOD, TSS) is fundamental. For farm managers, this objective is often the hardest because it can require investment in treatment systems or changes to feed/waste management. For skippers, it can mean choosing sites and routes that avoid fragile environments and adhering to discharge rules.

Paragraph 2 — Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) practicalities
RAS reduce water withdrawals by treating and reusing process water. Practically, RAS components include mechanical filtration (screening and settling), biological filtration (biofilters for ammonia conversion), solids removal (drum filters, clarifiers), and disinfection (UV or ozone). For smaller operations, a hybrid approach (partial recirculation) can deliver major water savings while keeping capital costs manageable. Key operational aspects: maintain consistent flow rates, backflush and clean mechanical filters to capture solids for valorization, monitor nitrate levels to avoid chronic toxicity, and maintain biofilter media and oxygenation to prevent nitrification failure. Document setpoints, maintenance schedules, and spare part inventories; these form part of the evidence you show for sustainable water use.

Paragraph 3 — Water treatment and effluent management
Effluent management includes sedimentation basins, constructed wetlands, or polishing treatment (sand filters, denitrifying bioreactors) depending on discharge quality and local permit requirements. For nearshore operations, benthic impact risks require effective solids capture and containment. Capture sludge in dedicated tanks and treat it for downstream reuse (compost, fertilizer) or anaerobic digestion. For land-based farms, ensure permit-aligned discharge parameters and maintain a sampling program (monthly at minimum) with lab certificates. Keep logs of corrective actions if thresholds are exceeded and record any permit communications.

Paragraph 4 — Simple monitoring regime and KPIs for water objectives
Set an operation-level water KPI dashboard: m3 water used per kg produced, % recirculated, mg/L of total nitrogen and phosphorus in discharge, and solids capture rate (kg/day). For skippers: map and avoid sensitive inshore areas and keep a log of any discharges or operations that could affect water quality. For Ibrahim: implement a daily visual check of clarifiers, weekly lab sampling cross-checked with online sensors (e.g., ammonia probes), and a documented SOP for responding to exceedances (reduce feed, increase solids removal, notify management). Practical and consistent sampling and quick corrective actions are what auditors want to see.

7) Transition to a circular economy (sludge capture, biogas, upcycling)
Paragraph 1 — What circular economy means for aquaculture operations
A circular economy in aquaculture focuses on turning waste streams into resources, reducing inputs and valorizing by-products. This means recovering nutrients, converting sludge and uneaten feed into valuable products (biogas, compost, feed additives), and designing systems that minimize single-use materials. Practically, a circular approach reduces waste disposal costs and creates new revenue or cost savings (e.g., energy from biogas). For managers seeking financing, demonstrating a pathway to circularity (current capture rate, planned upgrades, projected revenue or cost-offsets) is persuasive.

Paragraph 2 — Sludge capture and pre-treatment for valorization
Capture solids before discharge using drum filters, settling tanks and sludge dewatering. Once captured, stabilize sludge via composting, drying, or anaerobic digestion. For anaerobic digestion, pre-treat high-water-content sludge to improve gas yields, and consider co-digestion with other organic wastes (manure, food waste). For small operators, low-tech drying beds combined with composting can produce soil amendments for local agriculture. Document mass balances: kg solids input, percent moisture after dewatering, and final product yield. This documentation supports the “substantial contribution” to circular economy in the evidence file.

Paragraph 3 — Biogas and energy recovery practicalities
Biogas produced from sludge yields renewable energy that can offset grid electricity or generator fuel. A practical implementation plan includes sizing the digester to the volume of sludge, ensuring constant feedstock quality, and installing a biogas engine or upgraded burner. Maintenance regimes for digesters, gas cleaning and storage are critical and must be included in SOPs. Conduct a simple cost-benefit estimate: capital cost vs. saved fuel and potential gate fees for co-digestion. For banks, a well-documented biogas business case (with expected energy yields and payback period) demonstrates a strong route toward both circularity and GHG reduction.

Paragraph 4 — Upcycling by-products into feed, fertilizer or cosmetics
Many aquaculture by-products (heads, bones, viscera) can be rendered into fish meal or oils, processed for pet food, fertilizer or specialty products (collagen for cosmetics). Work with processors who meet food safety requirements and can issue traceability documentation. For on-site simplification, establish a sealed, refrigerated storage area for by-products and a weekly collection contract. For small operators, cooperative arrangements (where several farms consolidate by-products for a single processor) can be effective. Keep transaction records and invoices showing volumes sold or valorized; these become core proof for Taxonomy reporting for the circular economy objective.

8) Pollution prevention and control (zero runoff systems, chemical use reduction)
Paragraph 1 — Pollution prevention essentials and risk zones
Pollution prevention under the Taxonomy covers reducing discharges of chemicals, antibiotics, excess nutrients and solid waste that can harm ecosystems. Preventing pollution is often more cost-effective than remediation. Practically, it requires source-control measures (better feeding to reduce waste, substitution of harmful chemicals), containment (proper bunds and storage), and treatment. For managers, prevention is also about training: staff must know safe handling, dosing and disposal procedures and be able to record every chemical use and incident.

Paragraph 2 — Zero-runoff system designs and practical constraints
A zero-runoff approach aims to prevent any untreated effluent from leaving the site — achieved via treatment loops, recirculation, and holding ponds sized for stormwater and process upsets. Where full zero-runoff is not feasible, the operational goal is to ensure that all discharges meet permit standards by pre-treating effluent and having emergency containment for spills. Implement physical measures: impermeable surfaces around chemical storerooms, bunds around fuel tanks, spill kits on site, and controlled drainage that routes all runoff through treatment. Keep schematics of drainage, dates of maintenance and spill-response drill logs.

Paragraph 3 — Reducing chemical use and safer alternatives
Replace high-impact chemicals with safer alternatives and reduce prophylactic antibiotic use through better husbandry and biosecurity. For cleaning and disinfection, consider mechanical cleaning combined with less toxic agents (acetic or peracetic acid alternatives) where allowed. Use targeted dosing systems that measure water quality and dose only when needed. Track each chemical’s usage in a chemical logbook (date, product, quantity, purpose, person responsible). This logbook is essential evidence and helps identify opportunities to reduce consumption. For Ibrahim, maintain SDS (safety data sheets), have PPE checklists and run annual training updates for safe handling.

Paragraph 4 — Incident reporting and corrective actions
Even with prevention systems, incidents occur. A clear incident reporting procedure must exist: immediate containment, sample collection for lab analysis, notification to authorities if required, root-cause analysis, corrective actions and follow-up monitoring. Keep incident records with timestamps, photos and a remediation plan. Regularly analyze incident trends to modify SOPs or infrastructure — for instance, recurring chemical spills at a loading area may indicate the need for a covered loading bay or different transfer equipment. Demonstrable corrective action and learning are key to satisfying DNSH checks under the Taxonomy.

9) Protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems (fallowing, benthic sensors, seagrass metrics)
Paragraph 1 — Biodiversity as a central and cross-cutting Taxonomy objective
Biodiversity protection is a high-priority objective in the Taxonomy because aquaculture interacts directly with coastal and freshwater ecosystems. Protection requires both avoiding harm (preventing escapes, reducing nutrient loading and seabed impacts) and proactively restoring habitats where feasible (fallowing, seagrass restoration). For managers, demonstrating biodiversity protection includes documented site assessments, benthic monitoring, and adaptive management measures.

Paragraph 2 — Fallowing, stocking density and escape prevention
Fallowing (scheduled periods where sites are empty) helps benthic recovery by allowing organic matter to break down and benthic communities to re-establish. Implement a fallowing plan with defined lengths depending on species, biomass and local conditions, and document pre- and post-fallowing benthic surveys. Control stocking density to reduce organic loading and fish stress (which reduces disease risk and chemical use). Escape prevention is essential: maintain nets, inspect frames and moorings, and have rapid response plans for recapture. Keep maintenance and inspection logs with dates and personnel responsible; these logs are simple but powerful evidence for auditors.

Paragraph 3 — Benthic sensors, monitoring and simple metrics
Deploy benthic and water-column monitoring: sediment oxygen probes, redox potential sensors, and periodic grab samples for organic carbon and macrofauna counts. In coastal farms, use a grid of sediment sampling points and track indicators like sediment sulfide levels or macrofaunal diversity indices. For managers without access to advanced labs, partnerships with local universities or labs can give access to reliable analyses. Also use remote imaging (drop cameras) to visually document benthic condition before and after major events. The data should feed into an adaptive management loop: if benthic scores decline, reduce feeding, increase fallowing or relocate pens.

Paragraph 4 — Seagrass, habitat restoration and stakeholder engagement
Habitat restoration, such as seagrass replanting, can be part of a biodiversity contribution but requires careful planning and permits. Document baseline habitat conditions, engage local stakeholders (fishers, conservation groups) and use simple success metrics: area replanted (m2), percent survival at set intervals, and improvements in local biodiversity indices. For all operations, maintain a stakeholder engagement log showing consultations, agreements and any co-management arrangements. This transparency about local impacts and restoration actions helps meet both DNSH and social-minimum safeguard expectations in the Taxonomy.

PART B — Structured Learning Outcomes and Requirements

1. Comprehensive Knowledge Requirements (EQF Level 5 standard — multiple pages)
Introductory paragraph — what this section delivers
This section defines the detailed knowledge that learners at EQF Level 5 must acquire. At Level 5, learners need comprehensive, specialized knowledge and an ability to interpret and apply that knowledge to management and supervisory tasks, including planning, coordinating, and adapting procedures. The knowledge below is tailored to aquaculture contexts and is oriented toward immediate practical application: audits, KPI-setting, designing SOPs, managing upgrades, and interacting with lenders and buyers on Taxonomy claims.

Knowledge domain 1 — EU Taxonomy fundamentals and legal architecture
Learners must understand the purpose and legal architecture of the EU Taxonomy: the 2020 regulation establishing the framework, the role of delegated acts and technical screening criteria (TSC), and how NACE codes and sectoral mappings determine applicable criteria. They need to know the three-step alignment test (substantial contribution; DNSH; minimum safeguards), the linkage with financial reporting regimes (CSRD) and sustainable finance disclosures (SFDR), and the practical consequences for financing and procurement. Managers should be able to explain, in plain language, what “substantial contribution” and “DNSH” mean, and where to find the official TSCs for aquaculture or related activities. They must also understand that TSCs can be numeric thresholds (energy intensity, % waste valorized) or qualitative tests (e.g., best available techniques), and that delegated acts can update the criteria.

Knowledge domain 2 — The six environmental objectives in operational terms
Learners must possess an operational understanding of each objective: what constitutes a substantial contribution for that objective in aquaculture operations; typical DNSH risks and mitigation measures; relevant measurements (e.g., kWh/kg, kg N discharged per kg produced, % solids captured) and where to locate authoritative threshold values. They must be able to construct a simple objective-by-objective matrix for their operation that lists the activity, potential contributions, data required, known risks, and mitigation options. This includes being able to identify which objectives are most relevant to different parts of the operation (e.g., hatchery vs. grow-out vs. processing).

Knowledge domain 3 — Operational metrics, measurement methods and uncertainty
Managers must understand measurement types, the difference between direct metering and proxy measures, sampling frequencies, and acceptable calibration/QA for meters and labs. Specific examples include energy metering protocols (hourly records, sub-metering), fuel logging standards (trip logs with start/end readings), water sampling best practices (grab vs. composite samples), and sludge mass-balance calculation methods. Learners should also appreciate measurement uncertainty, when to use statistical averaging, and how to document sample chains and meter calibrations to make evidence defensible for auditors. They must be capable of designing a monitoring plan with sampling frequencies and responsibilities that address Taxonomy reporting needs.

Knowledge domain 4 — Technologies and methods for achieving objectives
Learners must have in-depth knowledge of key technologies and methods that meet Taxonomy objectives: RAS components, biofilters, solids removal equipment, solar PV and battery systems sizing and maintenance, feed management and sensorized feeding, anaerobic digestion basics for sludge valorization, denitrification systems, constructed wetlands, and mooring and cage design choices for adaptation. This includes understanding capital vs. operational costs, typical performance ranges (e.g., % water recirculated achievable), and maintenance demands. For each technology, learners must be able to specify the expected KPIs that would indicate success (e.g., % reduction in kWh/kg, % reduction in kg N discharged).

Knowledge domain 5 — Management systems, SOPs and evidence collection
Managers must know how to create and maintain management systems that produce the documentary evidence required: standardized SOPs for operations and maintenance; incident reporting templates; monitoring and calibration logs; training and competence records; investment plans with projected KPIs; and stakeholder engagement documentation. They must be able to implement a simple “evidence file” structure and demonstrate chain-of-custody for laboratory results and meter calibrations. Understanding the expectations around transparency, traceability and data integrity is crucial for meaningful Taxonomy claims.

Knowledge domain 6 — Risk assessment, DNSH analysis and adaptive management
At Level 5, learners must be competent in conducting DNSH-style risk assessments: identifying activities that could harm other objectives, mapping likelihood and consequence, and designing mitigation measures with measurable outcomes. They must understand adaptive management cycles (monitor — evaluate — adjust) and be able to lead or coordinate those cycles with clear decision gates and corrective-action triggers. This includes being able to prepare simple risk registers that link to operational SOPs and investment plans.

Knowledge domain 7 — Finance, business cases and communication
Managers should understand how Taxonomy alignment affects financing and procurement: what lenders and buyers typically request (evidence file, KPI dashboards, investment plans), how to prepare a simple cost-benefit for an upgrade (capex, opex, payback), and how to present Taxonomy claims in plain language for buyers and banks. They should be able to build a one-page Taxonomy summary for their operation showing aligned activities, KPIs and required next steps. Communication skills include presenting to non-technical audiences, preparing on-deck posters and checklists, and handling buyer or lender follow-up questions.

Knowledge domain 8 — Social and governance minimum safeguards
Learners must know the concept of minimum safeguards (respect for human rights and labour standards) and how to document compliance in practical ways: contracts, payroll records, work-time logs, safety training records, and grievance mechanisms. For smaller operations, simple documented policies and employee acknowledgements are often sufficient as evidence of observance of minimum safeguards.

Knowledge domain 9 — Ongoing compliance and review
Finally, managers must know the need for periodic review (every 6–12 months) of Taxonomy-relevant data, the delegated acts that may change TSCs and the need to update evidence files. They must be able to set up a review calendar, identify responsible persons for updates and train staff in changes to SOPs. This includes preparing for external audits by maintaining consistent, searchable records.

2. Practical and Cognitive Skills (detailed bullet actions they must take)
Introductory paragraph — skills framing
At EQF Level 5 the skills profile combines practical execution with supervisory judgement. Learners must be able to perform technical tasks and supervise and coordinate others to translate taxonomy criteria into practice. Below are concrete, action-oriented skill tasks, grouped by daily/weekly actions, monthly/quarterly activities and investment/finance preparation.

Daily/operational actions (what to do each day)
- Maintain daily energy and fuel logs: capture start/end meter readings for pumps, generators and main power points. For vessels, log engine hours, fuel used and catch. Train crew to sign off. Use a laminated on-deck poster with the three KPIs: fuel L/kg, kWh/kg, and feed kg/kg gain.
- Follow SOP for feeding: calibrate feeders at start of each shift, log feed dispensed per unit, inspect for uneaten feed at check points and adjust rations. Ibrahim must ensure feeds are stored dry and on pallets to avoid spoilage.
- Check water treatment and solids removal twice daily: visually inspect drum filters, confirm backflush cycles, and log clarifier levels; take immediate corrective action if clogging or overflow is detected.
- Run pre-departure environmental checks (for Lars): weather briefings tied to mooring risk, verify emergency spares onboard, ensure catch recording and bycatch sorting SOPs are followed to minimize discard losses and document mitigation measures.
- Chemical and waste handling: daily inventory checks and secured storage area inspection. Record any chemical use in a chemical logbook with SDS references and PPE use confirmation.

Weekly tasks (routine measurement and maintenance)
- Calibrate key meters weekly: energy clamp meters, flow sensors and temperature sensors. Keep calibration records with date, person and results.
- Sludge and solids accounting: weigh or estimate solids captured during weekly dewatering, record moisture content and mass balance against expected solids from feed and stocking density.
- Check backup power readiness: run generator under load (simulated outage) monthly to ensure it can handle critical loads; test battery backup performance and record state-of-health indicators.
- Feed and FCR review: calculate weekly FCR, flag deviations >10% from expected and trigger feed quality or management checks.
- Staff training and toolbox talks: short weekly sessions (15–30 minutes) on topics such as spill response, record-keeping, or new SOPs. Log attendance.

Monthly/quarterly monitoring and reporting
- Lab sampling program: monthly composite sampling of effluent for BOD/COD, total N, total P and TSS. Keep lab certificates and note any exceedances, corrective actions and follow-up tests.
- KPI dashboard updates: produce a monthly summary for manager review with kWh/kg, fuel L/kg, FCR, % solids captured, % energy from renewables, and any incidents. For Maria, use these for board/finance updates.
- DNSH checks: quarterly DNSH review where each activity is tested against the other five objectives for potential harm; update the DNSH risk register and mitigation status.
- Benthic/biological monitoring: quarterly or seasonal sediment sampling and biodiversity assessments depending on local conditions and regulatory requirements. Document findings and adjustments (fallowing decisions, feed changes).

Investment planning and finance readiness
- Map investments to KPI changes: for each proposed upgrade (e.g., solar + battery), provide expected reduction in kWh/kg, capex, opex change and simple payback. Provide sensitivity analysis for fuel and electricity price changes.
- Prepare an evidence summary for lenders: a one-page Taxonomy alignment statement, the evidence file structure, top 5 KPIs and a timeline of required actions to achieve alignment. Include permits and minimum safeguards documentation.
- Prioritization matrix: rank improvement projects by impact on KPI, cost, and time-to-implement. Use this to make supervisory decisions about prioritizing limited capital.

Cognitive and supervisory decision-making skills
- Translate technical screening criteria into practical thresholds: read the relevant TSC and convert a numeric threshold (e.g., X mg N/m3) into operational actions (e.g., reduce feed by Y% or add denitrification by date Z).
- Trade-off analysis: balance mitigation vs. biodiversity risks (e.g., if solar arrays require land use changes that affect habitats, craft mitigation such as habitat offsets).
- Incident triage: rapidly decide containment vs. shutdown in pollution events, escalate appropriately and document decisions and rationales.
- Stakeholder negotiation: communicate technical data and plans to buyers, lenders and regulators in plain language; produce simple visualizations showing before/after impacts.

3. Management and Supervisory Autonomy Outcomes (detailed scenarios they must manage)
Introductory paragraph — autonomy expectations
At EQF Level 5, learners must manage complex operational scenarios with supervisory autonomy: make decisions that affect multiple objectives, coordinate teams for implementation, and justify decisions with data. The following scenarios are realistic exercises managers must be able to lead from identification to resolution and reporting.

Scenario A — Rapid mitigation upgrade with DNSH check (example for Maria)
Situation: A lender offers concessional finance to install a rooftop solar + battery system if the farm can show it will reduce kWh/kg by 30% within a year. Maria must lead the project, but the farm sits near a protected wetland where construction could affect habitat. Tasks and expected outcomes:
- Conduct a rapid pre-feasibility: current energy baseline, roof area and expected kW yield, battery sizing for critical loads, capex and opex numbers.
- Perform a DNSH assessment: check whether construction or land use for panels will harm biodiversity; if yes, design mitigation (e.g., use floating or carport-mounted arrays away from the wetland, or arrange construction timing to avoid breeding seasons).
- Create the evidence package: energy models, installation photos, expected KPI change, documentation of mitigation and stakeholder consultation.
- Implementation and reporting: supervise installation, ensure commissioning tests, update KPI dashboard monthly and report progress to the lender with evidence of DNSH measures implemented.

Scenario B — Disease episode requiring adaptive management (example for Ibrahim)
Situation: A sudden disease outbreak raises mortalities and threatens increased antibiotic use, which could affect pollution and biodiversity metrics. Ibrahim must manage operations to control disease without violating DNSH for pollution prevention.
- Immediate operational actions: reduce stocking density where feasible, isolate affected units, intensify biosecurity, enhance monitoring of water quality and benthic impacts.
- Treatment strategy and documentation: apply treatments only as per veterinary guidance, document doses, dates and withdrawal times, and maintain sampling to ensure residues are below thresholds if required.
- Longer-term mitigation: improve husbandry to reduce antibiotic reliance (vaccination programs, feed improvements), and plan upgrades to solids capture to limit spread of antibiotics in effluent.
- Reporting and learning: prepare an incident report with root-cause analysis, corrective actions, and an updated SOP to reduce recurrence. Present findings in a management meeting and update the DNSH risk register.

Scenario C — Vessel retrofit and buyer due diligence (example for Lars)
Situation: A buyer wants a supplier to commit to reduced scope-1 emissions for vessels. Lars must decide whether to retrofit the vessel with a fuel-efficient engine or hybrid assist. Tasks:
- Quick audit: log last 12 months of fuel use per trip, engine hours, catch volumes and average L/kg delivered.
- Cost/benefit and downtime planning: estimate retrofit cost, expected fuel savings, downtime and training needs for crew.
- Operational checks and water/biodiversity considerations: ensure any changes to handling or gear do not increase escape risk or bycatch.
- Implementation and buyer communication: prepare a simple one-page summary for the buyer showing expected L/kg reduction, timeline for retrofit and contingency plans during retrofit. After implementation, run a 3-month monitoring period and provide the buyer with signed fuel logs and KPI comparisons.

Scenario D — Sludge valorization business case with stakeholder negotiations (cross-persona)
Situation: The farm produces a significant amount of sludge and wants to install a small anaerobic digester, but neighbors are concerned about odors and truck traffic. The manager must secure permits, financing and community support.
- Technical and financial planning: present a biogas yield estimate, capital costs, projected energy offset and payback, and outline odor-control measures.
- Stakeholder engagement: hold a public meeting, present mitigation strategies (covered digesters, gas flaring, limited truck movements), and propose community benefits (reduced waste handling costs or shared energy access).
- Permit and risk management: prepare environmental impact assessments and operational controls accepted by local regulators (waste acceptance lists, monitoring plans).
- Implementation and monitoring: commission the digester with odor controls, monitor emissions and maintain community liaison logs showing complaints and responses.

Scenario E — Seasonal extreme weather adaptation (for all personas)
Situation: A predicted severe storm season requires the operation to prepare to reduce risk of escape, infrastructure damage and prolonged outages.
- Pre-season actions: check and replace mooring lines, service generators and batteries, secure spare nets, and ensure staff availability and emergency plans.
- Emergency operations: have a chain-of-command and rapid communication procedures; for vessels, prioritize recovery of vulnerable gear and ensure crew safety; for farms, begin controlled harvests if insurance or market conditions favor early harvest to reduce biomass at risk.
- Post-event recovery: perform damage assessments, benthic and environmental impact sampling, and a remediation plan (net repairs, fallowing decisions) and report to regulators and insurers with photographic evidence and logs.

Concluding guidance for assessment and continued learning
To achieve EQF Level 5 outcomes, trainees should complete practical assignments: 1) build a Taxonomy evidence file for their operation (mapping, baseline KPIs, DNSH register); 2) run a week-long baseline measurement campaign; 3) create a one-page Taxonomy alignment statement and a prioritized investment list with expected KPI impacts; and 4) lead a mock lender/buyer interrogation with a supervisor acting as the external stakeholder. Assessment should include demonstration of documentation, an oral explanation of decisions and a practical drill (e.g., emergency changeover to backup power). Maintain a learning log and plan quarterly refreshers to incorporate updates to technical screening criteria.

Appendix — Ready-to-use practical tools and templates (short descriptions)
- On-deck/onsite poster templates: top KPIs (fuel L/kg, kWh/kg, FCR), emergency phone numbers, and three-step decision trees for spills, heatwave or power outage. Laminated and displayed prominently.
- Simple daily log templates: energy, fuel, feed, water intake/outflow, solids captured; signature field for accountability.
- Monthly KPI dashboard template: visual sheet with trend lines for the past 12 months and variance commentary.
- DNSH risk register template: activity — potential harm — likelihood — consequence — mitigation — owner — evidence.
- Investment one-page: description, cost, expected KPI delta, payback and DNSH considerations.
- Incident report template: date/time, event summary, immediate action, samples taken, lab results, corrective actions, and follow-up.

Final note — pathway to implementation
Start with a pragmatic baseline measurement week, create the evidence folder structure, and pick one quick-win (e.g., LED lighting, fuel log poster, or feed calibration SOP). Use the priority matrix to sequence larger investments (solar, RAS upgrades, digester), and ensure each decision is documented against the Taxonomy framework. Maintain clear, plain-language communication for crew, buyers and lenders — the Taxonomy is ultimately about credible evidence, and your best asset is consistent, well-documented operation and transparent reporting.
