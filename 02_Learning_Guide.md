## 1. Comprehensive Knowledge Requirements

### 1.1 What is the EU Taxonomy?

The EU Taxonomy is the European Union’s official classification system for environmentally sustainable economic activities. In simple terms, it is a common rulebook that helps businesses, investors, banks, and public authorities decide what can genuinely be called “green” in economic and financial terms. It was introduced through Regulation (EU) 2020/852, usually called the Taxonomy Regulation, adopted in 2020 as part of the wider European Green Deal and the EU’s push to move capital toward sustainable investment. Before the Taxonomy, many companies claimed to be sustainable using their own definitions, and many investors funded projects that looked positive on paper but did not always deliver real environmental improvement. The Taxonomy was created to solve that problem by setting a shared, science-based framework. For aquaculture professionals, this matters because it changes how environmental performance is described, documented, and financed. A farm can no longer simply state that it is “eco-friendly”; it increasingly needs to demonstrate, with evidence, how its activities meet specific environmental criteria.

The Taxonomy does not automatically certify an entire company as green. Instead, it assesses whether specific economic activities make a substantial contribution to one or more environmental objectives, do no significant harm to the others, and meet minimum social safeguards. This is a very important distinction for aquaculture operators. A farm may have one activity that aligns strongly with the Taxonomy, such as a recirculating aquaculture system that saves water, while another activity may still need improvement, such as fossil-fuel-dependent power generation or poor waste segregation. The framework is therefore practical and operational: it pushes businesses to look closely at each part of production. In an aquaculture setting, this means examining energy systems, hatchery water use, feed efficiency, seabed impact, chemical use, sludge handling, and biodiversity management as separate but connected operational areas. The Taxonomy is not just an environmental checklist; it is a management tool for directing investment and operational improvement.

Who does it affect? Directly, it affects large companies and financial market participants that must report how much of their turnover, capital expenditure, or operating expenditure is Taxonomy-aligned. Indirectly, it affects smaller businesses, including many aquaculture farms, because banks, investors, grant authorities, buyers, insurers, and public funding bodies increasingly ask for evidence of sustainable performance using Taxonomy logic. This is particularly relevant in the EU funding context. Programs such as EMFAF, LIFE, Horizon Europe, and European Investment Bank green loans all place growing value on measurable environmental outcomes. A fish farm manager applying for support to install a solar-wind hybrid system, upgrade to RAS, or create nutrient recovery from sludge will be in a stronger position if the proposal clearly shows which Taxonomy objective is being supported, what criteria are met, and what evidence is available. In practical terms, the Taxonomy helps connect on-farm improvements to external finance.

To classify an activity as environmentally sustainable, the Taxonomy uses four main conditions. First, the activity must contribute substantially to at least one of the six environmental objectives. Second, it must do no significant harm, often shortened to DNSH, to any of the other five objectives. Third, it must comply with minimum social safeguards, such as responsible labor and governance standards. Fourth, it must meet the technical screening criteria adopted by the European Commission for the relevant activity. This is where many practitioners initially feel overwhelmed, because “technical screening criteria” sounds abstract and legalistic. In reality, it means measurable tests. For example, a farm may need to show reduced emissions, improved water reuse rates, discharge control, biodiversity monitoring records, or climate risk assessments. For skilled aquaculture professionals, the key message is this: the EU Taxonomy is not about paperwork for its own sake. It is about proving, through records, systems, and measurable farm practices, that sustainability claims are real, credible, and investment-ready.

### 1.2 The Six Environmental Objectives

The EU Taxonomy is built around six environmental objectives, and understanding them is essential because every sustainability action on an aquaculture farm can be linked to one or more of these objectives. These objectives are defined in the Taxonomy Regulation and further detailed through delegated acts and technical screening criteria. The six objectives are: climate change mitigation, climate change adaptation, the sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources, the transition to a circular economy, pollution prevention and control, and the protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems. For aquaculture operators, these are not abstract policy headings. They correspond directly to major decisions made on the farm every day: how energy is produced, how feed is managed, how water is used, how waste is treated, how chemicals are controlled, and how the surrounding marine or freshwater environment is protected.

The first objective, climate change mitigation, is about reducing or avoiding greenhouse gas emissions. In aquaculture, this can include replacing diesel-powered systems with renewable electricity, improving feed conversion ratios to reduce resource intensity, using efficient pumps and aeration systems, electrifying workboats where feasible, and reducing energy losses in hatchery operations. The second objective, climate change adaptation, is about preparing operations for the impacts of climate change that are already happening or expected in the future. This includes measures such as stronger moorings for storms, temperature and oxygen monitoring systems for heatwaves, emergency water storage, backup energy systems, and planning for harmful algal blooms or changing salinity conditions. Mitigation reduces the farm’s contribution to climate change; adaptation increases the farm’s resilience to climate-related risks.

The third objective, sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources, is especially relevant to aquaculture because water is both the production medium and the surrounding ecosystem. Activities that reduce freshwater withdrawal, improve recirculation, treat effluent, monitor nutrient discharge, and protect receiving waters can contribute to this objective. The fourth objective, transition to a circular economy, focuses on keeping resources in use for longer, reducing waste, increasing reuse and recycling, and recovering value from by-products. For aquaculture, this may mean turning sludge into biogas, using fish by-products in feed or fertilizer chains, reducing packaging waste, repairing rather than replacing equipment, and designing procurement systems around resource efficiency. Circularity is particularly relevant because many farms generate organic by-products that can become inputs for other sectors.

The fifth objective, pollution prevention and control, deals with avoiding contamination of water, soil, air, and ecosystems. In aquaculture, this includes reducing nutrient runoff, managing chemical storage safely, minimizing antibiotic use through good husbandry, controlling microplastics and feed loss, and ensuring dead fish and hazardous waste are handled correctly. The sixth objective, protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems, is central for coastal, shellfish, and marine cage farming, but it also matters in inland systems. This objective covers habitat protection, seabed health, species interactions, restoration of sensitive ecosystems, and responsible siting and operation of farms. For example, benthic monitoring around cages, fallowing schedules, avoiding sensitive habitats, and supporting the recovery of Posidonia oceanica seagrass beds are all highly relevant actions. The legal basis gives structure, but for aquaculture professionals the practical meaning is more important: the six objectives together create a full environmental management map of the farm, from power supply to seabed condition.

### 1.3 Aquaculture Alignment — How a Real Farm Assesses, Audits, and Proves Alignment

Aquaculture alignment with the EU Taxonomy does not begin with legal interpretation; it begins with operational mapping. A real farm first needs to understand what activities it carries out and how those activities relate to the six environmental objectives. This means breaking the farm into practical functional areas, such as energy and infrastructure, hatchery and water systems, feeding operations, fish health management, waste handling, transport, packaging, and marine site management. In the CircuTech training structure, this is represented through operational zones: Zone A for energy and infrastructure, Zone B for water management in the hatchery, Zone C for waste processing, Zone D for seabed and biodiversity protection, and Zone E for synthesis and reporting. This zone-based approach is especially effective for professionals with strong practical knowledge because it starts from what they already know: where the equipment is, what the process does, what inputs go in, and what outputs or impacts come out. Alignment becomes easier when the Taxonomy is translated into a facility walk-through rather than a legal lecture.

The next step is assessment. The farm identifies which activities may substantially contribute to one or more Taxonomy objectives. For example, if the hatchery uses a recirculating aquaculture system that reuses more than 95% of water, this may support the objective on water and marine resources. If sludge is captured and transformed into biogas or agricultural fertilizer, this may support circular economy and pollution prevention. If climate-resilient moorings and continuous temperature sensors are installed to manage heat and storm risk, that may support climate adaptation. At this stage, the farm should collect baseline data. This may include diesel consumption in liters per month, electricity use in kWh per ton of fish produced, feed conversion ratio, daily water abstraction volume, percentage of water reused, nitrogen and phosphorus concentration in discharge, sludge volume recovered, mortality rate, antibiotic usage, storm-damage incidents, and benthic impact data. Without baseline data, the farm cannot prove improvement or alignment.

The third step is audit and evidence collection. This is where many businesses discover that they may already be doing sustainable work but have not documented it in a structured way. An alignment audit should check three things: substantial contribution, DNSH, and minimum safeguards. For substantial contribution, evidence may include technical specifications of renewable energy systems, metered energy data, laboratory results for treated discharge, maintenance logs of RAS filters, nutrient recovery records, and biodiversity monitoring reports. For DNSH, the farm must show that gains in one area do not create major harm in another. For example, a new waste valorization plant should not create uncontrolled emissions or unsafe handling conditions; a water-saving technology should not increase chemical concentration to harmful levels; and a biodiversity protection action should not be undermined by untreated discharge from another part of the facility. Evidence can include permits, environmental impact assessments, stock management records, chemical inventories, emergency plans, and monitoring protocols. In practice, the audit process often exposes weak points in recordkeeping rather than weak points in farming itself.

The final step is proof and presentation. Once the farm has assessed activities, collected baseline and performance data, and checked DNSH, it compiles a clear compliance file or sustainability action plan. This file should link each improvement to a Taxonomy objective, identify the technical criteria addressed, provide supporting evidence, and state what still needs improvement. For funding applications, this can be transformed into a structured investment case: problem, intervention, objective, evidence, expected environmental gain, monitoring method, and financing need. The process typically follows a practical sequence: assess compliance, document evidence, submit application, undergo expert verification, and receive funding. A farm manager who can clearly show that renewable energy reduced diesel use by 70%, that RAS lowered freshwater withdrawal by 95%, that nutrient runoff was eliminated through sludge capture, and that seabed monitoring confirms ecosystem recovery is in a far stronger position with funders and green lenders. Alignment, therefore, is not just about passing a sustainability test. It is about making the farm legible, credible, and investable.

### 1.4 Climate Change Mitigation — Solar-Wind Systems, Feed Conversion Optimization, Energy Efficiency Tools

Climate change mitigation in aquaculture means reducing the farm’s contribution to greenhouse gas emissions. For many aquaculture facilities, the largest direct and indirect emissions come from electricity and fuel use, feed production and use, transport, aeration and pumping, heating or cooling systems, and backup power generation. Diesel generators are a common example, especially in remote coastal or offshore locations where grid access is limited or unreliable. Replacing or reducing diesel dependency is therefore one of the clearest mitigation opportunities. In the context of Zone A, a solar-wind hybrid energy system can make a major difference. Solar panels generate power during daylight hours, while small-scale wind turbines can complement them in coastal areas where wind availability is often strong. When combined with battery storage and a smart energy management controller, these systems can reduce fuel costs, improve energy security, and cut emissions significantly. For a farm manager, the key question is not whether renewable energy is fashionable, but whether it lowers operating risk and improves long-term efficiency. In many cases, it does both.

To assess mitigation properly, a farm should start by building an energy and emissions profile. This means measuring how much diesel, electricity, and other fuels are used across the site and linking them to functions such as pumping, aeration, feeding equipment, vehicles, cold storage, grading lines, and lighting. A practical metric is energy use per ton of fish or shellfish produced, expressed for example as kWh/ton or liters diesel/ton. Once this baseline is known, mitigation measures can be prioritized. Solar-powered aeration, variable-speed pumps, efficient blowers, LED lighting, insulated pipework, automated feeding systems, and improved cold-chain equipment are all examples of technologies that can reduce energy demand. Some improvements are low-cost and fast, such as fixing leaks in compressed air systems, reducing unnecessary pump run-times, and scheduling high-load equipment to match renewable generation periods. Others are larger capital investments, such as hybrid generation systems or full equipment replacement. Under the EU Taxonomy, these actions become stronger when they are tied to measurable reductions in carbon intensity.

Feed conversion optimization is another major mitigation lever, and it is often underestimated because people tend to think of climate action only in terms of energy. Feed production carries a significant environmental footprint, and inefficient feeding increases both emissions and water pollution. Feed conversion ratio (FCR) is therefore not only a production efficiency metric; it is also a sustainability metric. If a farm improves FCR from 1.8 to 1.4, it means less feed is required per kilogram of fish produced, reducing upstream feed-related emissions and lowering nutrient losses into the system. Practical tools for this include demand feeders, camera-based feeding control, biomass estimation software, dissolved oxygen-linked feeding adjustments, and staff training in feed observation. Better feed storage also matters, since spoiled or humid feed increases waste. In a mitigation-focused management plan, improved FCR should be documented as a resource and emissions reduction measure, not only as a cost-saving exercise.

A strong climate mitigation strategy combines technology, monitoring, and management behavior. Installing a solar-wind system without energy metering gives weak evidence. Buying efficient pumps without training staff on operating schedules reduces impact. Claiming lower emissions without recording diesel reductions or electricity substitution will not satisfy a serious audit or investor review. Farms should therefore use practical indicators such as percentage of renewable energy in total consumption, annual diesel liters avoided, electricity use per production unit, FCR trends, and CO2 equivalent reductions where possible. Even if the farm does not conduct full carbon accounting at the start, it can build a clear mitigation story through operational data. In the EU funding context, this matters greatly. EMFAF, LIFE, Horizon Europe, and green loan providers respond well to projects that show measurable emission reduction, clear technical logic, and a realistic implementation plan. For aquaculture professionals, mitigation is best understood as better engineering, better feeding, and better monitoring that together make the farm cleaner, cheaper to run, and more financeable.

### 1.5 Climate Change Adaptation — DNSH Criteria, Infrastructure Resilience, Temperature Monitoring Systems

Climate change adaptation is about preparing the farm to function safely and effectively under changing environmental conditions. Unlike mitigation, which focuses on reducing emissions, adaptation focuses on risk management and resilience. Aquaculture is especially exposed to climate impacts because production depends directly on water temperature, oxygen levels, current patterns, storm events, salinity, disease pressure, and ecosystem stability. Heatwaves can reduce oxygen and increase mortality. More intense storms can damage cages, moorings, pontoons, and power infrastructure. Irregular rainfall can affect freshwater availability and salinity. Harmful algal blooms may become more frequent or shift seasonally. For aquaculture managers, adaptation is not a distant policy issue. It is already present in day-to-day decisions about site maintenance, stocking density, monitoring systems, emergency planning, and investment priorities. Under the EU Taxonomy, adaptation is recognized as a separate environmental objective because an activity can be well-managed environmentally yet still be highly vulnerable to climate shocks. A sustainable farm must not only reduce its impact; it must also remain resilient.

A core concept linked to adaptation in the Taxonomy is climate risk assessment. Before claiming that an adaptation measure contributes substantially, the farm should identify relevant physical climate risks over the short, medium, and long term. These may include marine heatwaves, stronger winds, wave damage, flooding, drought, changing pathogen patterns, water quality variability, or coastal erosion. This assessment does not need to be overly academic, but it must be systematic. A practical approach is to list each major climate hazard, identify which asset or operation it affects, estimate likelihood and consequence, and then define a response. For example: hazard—summer water temperature spikes; affected operation—juvenile rearing tanks; consequence—stress, low appetite, mortality; response—continuous temperature and dissolved oxygen monitoring, alarm thresholds, backup aeration, reduced stocking density, and heat contingency protocol. This risk-based method is highly relevant to DNSH as well. If a farm installs new adaptation infrastructure, it must ensure the solution does not create significant harm elsewhere. For example, reinforcing shoreline structures should not damage sensitive habitats, and emergency pumping systems should not increase pollution or noise beyond acceptable limits.

Infrastructure resilience is one of the clearest adaptation measures in aquaculture. In Zone A, climate-resilient moorings provide a practical case. Stronger anchor systems, corrosion-resistant materials, better load calculations, and more frequent inspection schedules reduce the risk of storm-related equipment failure and fish escape. Resilient infrastructure also includes elevated electrical systems in flood-prone zones, protected cable routing, backup generators placed outside inundation areas, insulated hatchery spaces, water storage buffers, and storm-rated buildings. In land-based systems, adaptation may involve thermal control measures, improved drainage, backup filtration capacity, and water source diversification. The value of these measures should be demonstrated through records such as engineering specifications, maintenance plans, incident reports, and updated emergency response procedures. A resilient farm is not one that never faces climate stress; it is one that can absorb the stress, continue operating, and recover quickly without major environmental or economic damage.

Temperature monitoring systems are a key practical adaptation tool because temperature is one of the most influential variables in aquaculture performance and risk. Continuous sensors placed in tanks, raceways, intake lines, and marine cages can provide real-time data to support decisions on feeding, oxygen management, biomass movement, harvesting, and emergency intervention. In modern systems, these sensors can be linked to alarms on mobile devices, cloud-based dashboards, or automated control systems for aeration and water exchange. Data trends are especially useful: not just current temperature, but rate of change, seasonal anomalies, and correlation with mortality, appetite, and dissolved oxygen. A professional adaptation plan should define trigger thresholds, for example when water exceeds a species-specific thermal comfort range or when oxygen drops below a set level during warm periods. Combined with weather forecasts and historical farm records, these systems help managers act before losses occur. Under the EU Taxonomy, adaptation becomes credible when it is planned, risk-based, documented, and measurable. For aquaculture professionals, this means moving from reacting to bad weather after the damage happens to managing climate risk as a normal part of operational supervision.

### 1.6 Water and Marine Resources — Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS), Water Treatment Cycles, Discharge Monitoring

The objective of sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources is particularly important for aquaculture because water is not only an input; it is the production environment itself. Poor water management affects fish health, operating costs, regulatory compliance, and public trust. In practical terms, this objective asks whether a farm uses water efficiently, protects receiving water bodies, and avoids degrading marine or freshwater ecosystems. In the CircuTech framework, this is represented strongly by Zone B, the hatchery, where a recirculating aquaculture system can transform how water is managed. A traditional flow-through system may draw large volumes of water continuously and discharge much of it after limited use. By contrast, a well-designed RAS treats and reuses water repeatedly, often achieving reuse rates above 95%. This reduces freshwater abstraction, lowers pressure on local water bodies, stabilizes growing conditions, and can improve biosecurity. For learners new to the Taxonomy, this is a good example of how a technology can directly support a specific EU environmental objective while also improving operational control.

A recirculating aquaculture system works by continuously cleaning and reconditioning water so that it can return to the tanks instead of being discharged. The treatment cycle typically includes solids removal, biofiltration, oxygenation or aeration, degassing, and often disinfection through UV or ozone. Mechanical filters remove uneaten feed and feces. Biofilters convert toxic ammonia from fish waste into less harmful nitrate through nitrification. Degassing units remove excess carbon dioxide, while oxygen cones or diffusers restore dissolved oxygen to safe levels. Additional polishing steps may include foam fractionation, drum filtration, or denitrification depending on the species and system design. The practical value for farm staff is clear: more stable temperature, oxygen, and water quality conditions can improve survival and growth, while lower water intake reduces dependence on external water sources. However, RAS also requires disciplined monitoring and maintenance. If filters clog, biofilter bacteria fail, or sensors drift out of calibration, the system can deteriorate quickly. Therefore, sustainable water management is not simply a matter of installing technology; it depends on operational competence.

Discharge monitoring is the proof side of water sustainability. Even farms with advanced treatment systems must show what leaves the site, not just what happens inside it. A farm aligned with this objective should monitor key discharge parameters such as total suspended solids, biochemical oxygen demand or chemical oxygen demand where relevant, nitrogen, phosphorus, pH, temperature, salinity, and in some cases pathogen or chemical traces. In marine cage systems, the approach differs because discharge is diffuse rather than pipe-based, but monitoring still matters through feed loss tracking, nutrient load estimation, current analysis, and benthic impact assessments. The purpose is to show that the farm is not transferring environmental cost downstream. In a hatchery using RAS, records should include total water withdrawal, recirculation percentage, sludge capture volume, filter maintenance logs, and analytical results from any remaining discharge stream. If the farm claims reduced pressure on water resources, it should be able to demonstrate this with monthly or seasonal data, not only with supplier brochures or general statements.

For Taxonomy alignment, managers should think in terms of water balance and protection. How much water enters the farm? How much is reused? What quality is maintained inside the system? What quality leaves the system? What happens in drought, flood, or contamination incidents? A strong management plan links infrastructure, procedures, and monitoring. For example, a hatchery may document 97% water recirculation, 40% lower freshwater withdrawal compared with baseline, automated alarms for dissolved oxygen and ammonia, weekly calibration of probes, and monthly effluent testing within permit limits. In marine systems, the plan may include site carrying-capacity assessment, hydrodynamic review, feed management protocols, and seabed monitoring to ensure the wider marine environment remains protected. For aquaculture professionals, this objective is best understood as disciplined water stewardship. Good water management is not only a technical requirement; it is one of the strongest indicators that the farm is operating responsibly, efficiently, and in line with future funding and regulatory expectations.

### 1.7 Circular Economy Transition — Sludge-to-Biogas, Fertilizer Byproducts, Packaging Waste Reduction

The transition to a circular economy means moving away from the old linear model of “take, make, use, dispose” and toward a system where resources are kept in use for as long as possible, waste is minimized, and by-products are turned into valuable inputs. In aquaculture, this objective is highly relevant because many outputs currently treated as waste still contain useful material, energy, or nutrients. In Zone C, waste processing provides a practical and visible example of circular economy thinking in action. Organic sludge from tanks, filters, or settling systems contains nutrients and organic matter that can be recovered rather than discarded. If captured properly, this sludge can be used in anaerobic digestion to produce biogas or processed into fertilizer products for agriculture. This reduces disposal costs, lowers pollution risk, and creates value from a material that would otherwise be a liability. For professionals unfamiliar with policy language, circular economy simply means: stop paying to throw away materials that could become useful outputs.

Sludge-to-biogas is a particularly strong example because it links waste recovery, energy production, and pollution reduction. In an anaerobic digestion process, organic material is broken down by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen, producing biogas rich in methane and a digestate that may have fertilizer value if managed correctly and in compliance with applicable rules. For aquaculture farms, sludge can be co-digested with other organic waste streams, such as food processing residues or agricultural manure, if local infrastructure and regulation allow. This cross-sector cooperation is a realistic opportunity highlighted in regional practice and policy discussions. A farm that cannot install its own digester may still separate and stabilize sludge for transfer to an external biogas facility. To support a circular economy claim, the farm should quantify sludge captured, percentage diverted from disposal, biogas produced or energy substituted, and nutrient recovery outcomes. Even simple records such as kilograms of dry sludge, transport notes, and partnership agreements with processors help demonstrate that waste is being valorized rather than dumped.

Fertilizer byproducts offer another pathway for circular use of aquaculture residues. Sludge or processed organic fractions can contain nitrogen, phosphorus, and organic carbon that may benefit soil if treated, stabilized, and approved for use according to the relevant standards. This is particularly important in a European context where phosphorus is a strategic resource and nutrient recovery is increasingly encouraged. A circular farm should examine whether its waste streams can serve agriculture, horticulture, composting systems, or industrial biomass chains. However, circularity does not mean using waste carelessly. Quality, contamination risk, moisture content, storage conditions, odor management, and traceability all matter. If the by-product contains residues of chemicals, excess salts, or unsuitable pathogens, treatment and control become essential. Therefore, a circular economy approach requires both innovation and discipline. The goal is not merely to move waste off-site; it is to create a safe and credible secondary resource flow.

Packaging waste reduction is another often overlooked but practical part of circular transition. Aquaculture operations use feed bags, plastic containers, ropes, nets, strapping, polystyrene boxes, liners, and transport packaging. A circular plan should therefore include purchasing choices, segregation systems, reusable alternatives, supplier take-back schemes, and repair or refurbishment of equipment where possible. Examples include switching to reusable fish transport crates, reducing single-use plastics in internal logistics, selecting feed suppliers with recyclable packaging, and introducing designated storage for sorted plastics, metals, and hazardous items. Waste audits can identify what is generated most often and where losses occur. For instance, if a farm uses 10,000 feed sacks per year, even a partial shift to bulk delivery or recyclable packaging creates visible impact. Circular economy alignment in aquaculture is strongest when the farm can show that by-products and materials are tracked, recovered, and reintegrated into productive use. This is not only environmentally positive; it also demonstrates operational maturity to funders, auditors, and business partners.

### 1.8 Pollution Prevention & Control — Zero-Runoff Systems, Chemical Inventory Management, AMR Drug Use Reduction

Pollution prevention and control focuses on stopping harmful substances, nutrients, residues, and waste from damaging air, soil, water, or living organisms. In aquaculture, pollution can occur in many forms: nutrient-rich effluent, escaped feed, sludge discharge, fuel spills, disinfectant misuse, antibiotic residues, antifoulant runoff, plastic debris, and poor dead-fish handling. This objective is closely linked to the circular economy objective, but it is not the same. Circular economy asks how waste can be reduced and reused; pollution prevention asks whether harmful releases are being avoided and controlled. In Zone C, the target of zero nutrient runoff through closed-loop systems is a strong example. If a farm captures solids, treats process water, prevents overflow losses, and ensures no untreated nutrient-rich effluent reaches surrounding waters, it is directly addressing this objective. For aquaculture professionals, this objective is not about perfection in theory. It is about having systems that prevent routine pollution and protocols that quickly stop accidental releases before they become environmental incidents.

Zero-runoff systems are especially relevant in hatcheries, land-based grow-out units, and processing-linked aquaculture facilities. These systems aim to prevent untreated wastewater and nutrient loads from leaving the site. Practical elements include sealed drainage design, sludge settling and capture, filter backwash management, emergency retention tanks, overflow alarms, and controlled discharge points. In some facilities, process water can be reused internally after treatment, while concentrated waste fractions are removed for specialized handling. The term “zero runoff” should be used carefully and honestly; it should mean that no uncontrolled nutrient-laden release occurs, not merely that water use is lower. To support this claim, a manager should be able to show engineering layouts, maintenance schedules, discharge records, and event logs demonstrating that overflow, leakage, and uncontrolled bypass have been prevented. Pollution control is strongest when it is built into the system design rather than added later as a corrective measure.

Chemical inventory management is another essential aspect. Aquaculture facilities often store disinfectants, pH adjusters, fuels, lubricants, treatment chemicals, cleaning agents, and laboratory reagents. If these materials are poorly labeled, stored without bunding, or used without updated records, pollution risk rises sharply. A professional chemical management system should include a complete inventory, safety data sheets, storage maps, container labeling, segregated storage by compatibility, spill response kits, disposal routes, and staff training records. Usage logs should identify who used what, when, where, and for what purpose. This helps not only with environmental safety but also with cost control and audit readiness. In practical terms, a farm should be able to answer simple supervisory questions immediately: Which chemicals are on site? Which are hazardous to water? Where are they stored? What is the expiry date? What happens if there is a spill? The ability to answer these questions is a direct indicator of pollution risk control.

AMR, or antimicrobial resistance, makes drug use reduction especially important. Overuse or misuse of antibiotics in any animal production system contributes to resistance risks that affect animal health, human health, and ecosystem health. In aquaculture, reducing antibiotic dependency should begin with prevention: strong biosecurity, good water quality, appropriate stocking density, vaccination where applicable, stress reduction, quarantine procedures, and early disease detection. If treatment is necessary, it should be targeted, prescribed, recorded, and reviewed rather than routine. Metrics such as number of antibiotic treatment events per production cycle, dosage per biomass, mortality trends, and post-treatment review findings can help farms monitor progress. The objective is not to deny treatment when animals need it, but to eliminate avoidable use and improve husbandry so that disease pressure is lower in the first place. Under the EU Taxonomy, pollution prevention and control becomes meaningful when a farm demonstrates that nutrient releases, hazardous substances, and antimicrobial risks are systematically reduced through design, records, and management discipline.

### 1.9 Biodiversity & Ecosystem Protection — Fallowing Schedules, Benthic Sensor Deployment, Posidonia oceanica Restoration Metrics

The protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems is the environmental objective most visibly connected to the farm’s relationship with the wider natural environment. In aquaculture, this includes the condition of the seabed or freshwater habitat beneath and around the farm, the status of nearby sensitive species and habitats, interactions with wild fauna, and the farm’s ability to avoid or reduce ecological disturbance. This objective is especially important for marine cage farming, shellfish production, lagoon systems, and coastal hatcheries, but inland aquaculture also has biodiversity responsibilities. Zone D in the CircuTech model focuses on the coastal seabed, where farm management decisions have direct consequences for benthic communities and habitat recovery. The objective is not simply to avoid legal non-compliance; it is to ensure that farm operation does not progressively degrade ecological function. For practitioners, this means knowing what habitats are present, what the farm’s pressure points are, and how to monitor and reduce impact over time.

Fallowing schedules are one of the most practical management tools for supporting ecosystem recovery. Fallowing means temporarily resting a production site or part of it after a production cycle so that the environment can recover from accumulated organic loading and disturbance. In marine cage aquaculture, this can reduce benthic enrichment, improve oxygen conditions in sediments, and support the return of benthic fauna and flora. A well-designed fallowing schedule is not random. It should be based on production intensity, current patterns, seabed sensitivity, monitoring results, and species-specific operational needs. The schedule may involve rotating cages, leaving sections unstocked for defined periods, coordinating stocking and harvesting windows, and documenting ecological recovery indicators before re-use. For a manager, fallowing is sometimes seen as lost production time, but in reality it is a strategic tool that protects site capacity, reduces long-term ecological risk, and supports evidence of responsible stewardship. Under a Taxonomy approach, fallowing becomes stronger when it is linked to monitoring results rather than simply done as a routine without assessment.

Benthic sensor deployment adds the measurement needed to make biodiversity management credible. Sensors and monitoring tools can track parameters such as dissolved oxygen near the seabed, redox potential, sediment condition, organic deposition, current movement, and in some systems even acoustic or visual indicators of habitat change. Remote sensing, grab sampling, diver surveys, underwater cameras, and GIS mapping can also be used depending on site conditions and budget. The purpose is to move from assumption to evidence. If a farm claims that seabed impacts are controlled, it should be able to show trends over time, identify hot spots, and demonstrate response actions when thresholds are exceeded. For example, if sensor and sediment data show organic enrichment beneath a cage array, the farm may need to reduce stocking density, adjust feeding, increase fallowing duration, or rotate cage positions. In EQF Level 5 terms, the supervisor must not only understand that monitoring exists, but also interpret results and trigger management decisions based on them.

Posidonia oceanica restoration is a strong Mediterranean example because this seagrass species is ecologically valuable and sensitive to disturbance. Posidonia meadows provide habitat, stabilize sediments, store carbon, and support biodiversity, but they are vulnerable to shading, physical damage, and poor water quality. Where aquaculture operates near these habitats, protection and restoration metrics become highly relevant. A farm may monitor seagrass coverage, shoot density, meadow edge stability, epiphyte load, light penetration, and distance between farm infrastructure and sensitive habitat. Restoration actions may include exclusion zones, relocation of anchoring systems, reduction of nutrient pressure, seabed recovery periods, and collaboration with marine ecologists on transplantation or recovery observation where appropriate. The key point is that biodiversity protection must be measurable. It is not enough to say that the farm “respects nature.” A credible biodiversity management plan identifies the habitat, measures its condition, tracks farm pressure, and records improvement or decline over time. For aquaculture professionals, this objective represents the highest level of environmental responsibility: operating productively while actively protecting the living system that makes aquaculture possible.

## 2. Practical and Cognitive Skills

After completing this module, the learner should be able to:

- Explain the purpose of the EU Taxonomy in clear operational language to staff, owners, auditors, investors, or funding bodies.
- Identify the legal origin of the Taxonomy in Regulation (EU) 2020/852 and describe its role in sustainable finance and environmental classification.
- Distinguish between a company being generally “sustainable” and a specific economic activity being “Taxonomy-aligned.”
- List and explain the six environmental objectives and match each one to real aquaculture operations.
- Interpret the concepts of substantial contribution, Do No Significant Harm (DNSH), minimum safeguards, and technical screening criteria in practical farm terms.
- Break an aquaculture facility into operational zones or functional areas for Taxonomy assessment.
- Map Zone A, B, C, D, and E activities to the correct environmental objectives.
- Conduct a baseline review of current farm performance using data on energy, water, waste, feed, chemicals, and biodiversity impact.
- Collect and organize evidence needed for a Taxonomy-related compliance review, including permits, maintenance records, metering data, laboratory results, and environmental monitoring records.
- Carry out a simple gap analysis to identify where current farm practices meet, partly meet, or do not yet meet sustainability requirements.
- Build a step-by-step Taxonomy alignment plan for a real farm improvement project.
- Prepare documentation suitable for green finance applications, including objective mapping, evidence summary, expected environmental outcomes, and verification needs.
- Calculate and interpret key performance indicators such as:
  - kWh per ton of product
  - liters of diesel per month or per production cycle
  - feed conversion ratio
  - percentage of renewable energy use
  - water recirculation percentage
  - freshwater abstraction volume
  - sludge recovery volume
  - discharge nutrient concentrations
  - antibiotic treatment frequency
  - biodiversity monitoring indicators
- Assess the mitigation value of replacing diesel generation with solar-wind hybrid systems.
- Identify energy efficiency opportunities in pumps, aeration, feeding systems, lighting, refrigeration, and process control.
- Evaluate feed management practices for their effect on both cost and emissions.
- Design a basic climate mitigation action plan with measurable energy and carbon-related targets.
- Perform a climate risk screening for aquaculture operations, including storms, heatwaves, flooding, salinity shifts, and harmful algal blooms.
- Apply adaptation thinking to infrastructure planning, including moorings, electrical systems, drainage, backup energy, and thermal protection.
- Use temperature and dissolved oxygen monitoring data to make operational decisions.
- Set trigger thresholds and response actions for climate-related risks affecting stock welfare and site stability.
- Explain how a Recirculating Aquaculture System works, including solids removal, biofiltration, aeration, oxygenation, and disinfection.
- Assess whether a hatchery or production unit is using water efficiently and identify opportunities to improve reuse and reduce discharge.
- Interpret water quality and discharge monitoring data in relation to permit compliance and environmental performance.
- Design a basic water stewardship plan that includes intake, reuse, treatment, and discharge control.
- Identify aquaculture by-products and waste streams with circular economy potential.
- Evaluate whether sludge can be directed toward biogas production, fertilizer recovery, or other valorization pathways.
- Conduct a simple waste audit covering organic waste, plastics, packaging, metals, and hazardous materials.
- Recommend packaging reduction and reuse measures suitable for farm logistics and procurement.
- Distinguish circular economy actions from pollution control actions while recognizing where they reinforce each other.
- Design or assess zero-runoff and closed-loop wastewater handling practices.
- Establish or improve a farm chemical inventory management system with traceability and spill prevention measures.
- Review antibiotic and veterinary treatment practices with reference to AMR risk reduction and preventive health management.
- Interpret the environmental implications of nutrient runoff, chemical misuse, and poor waste handling.
- Explain why fallowing is used and schedule it as part of seabed recovery and site management.
- Use benthic monitoring information to support decisions on stocking density, site rotation, and recovery periods.
- Recognize sensitive habitats, especially Posidonia oceanica in Mediterranean contexts, and identify protective management actions.
- Define measurable biodiversity indicators and include them in site supervision routines.
- Prepare a clear, evidence-based Taxonomy action plan to present to management, auditors, investors, or funding agencies.
- Coordinate with technical specialists, environmental consultants, and finance teams using accurate operational terminology.
- Supervise staff data collection so that environmental evidence is reliable, consistent, and audit-ready.
- Translate complex sustainability requirements into simple work instructions for farm teams.
- Evaluate whether a proposed investment is likely to strengthen Taxonomy alignment and funding eligibility.
- Support applications to EMFAF, LIFE, Horizon Europe, or EIB green finance by structuring environmental evidence correctly.

## 3. Management and Supervisory Autonomy Outcomes

### Scenario 1: Preparing a Green Investment Proposal for Farm Modernization
The learner must be able to independently lead the preparation of a farm modernization proposal aimed at securing EMFAF support or an EIB green loan. In this scenario, the farm plans to replace diesel generators with a solar-wind hybrid system, upgrade hatchery water management with high-recirculation RAS components, and install sludge capture for off-site biogas processing. The learner must map each investment to the correct EU Taxonomy objective, gather baseline and projected performance data, coordinate evidence collection across technical and administrative teams, identify DNSH risks, and compile a structured compliance file suitable for external review. This includes presenting expected reductions in diesel use, water abstraction, and nutrient discharge in a clear and defensible way.

### Scenario 2: Responding to Climate Risk at an Operational Site
The learner must be able to independently supervise climate adaptation planning after repeated storm damage and summer temperature stress events at a coastal site. In this scenario, the learner carries out a practical climate risk assessment, identifies vulnerabilities in moorings, electrical systems, temperature control, and emergency procedures, and introduces corrective measures such as climate-resilient moorings, sensor-based temperature alarms, backup aeration, and revised emergency response thresholds. The learner must justify these measures using adaptation logic, ensure they do not cause significant harm to other environmental objectives, assign responsibilities to staff, and monitor whether the site becomes more resilient over time.

### Scenario 3: Investigating and Correcting Pollution and Waste Management Weaknesses
The learner must be able to independently manage a situation where routine monitoring shows elevated nutrient levels in discharge water and poor segregation of sludge and packaging waste. In this scenario, the learner reviews system design, maintenance records, feeding practices, chemical use, and waste handling routines to identify the cause of the problem. The learner then implements corrective measures such as improved solids capture, discharge control, revised chemical storage procedures, staff retraining, and a circular waste segregation plan. The learner must document actions taken, verify that the pollution risk has been reduced, and ensure that the farm’s evidence base is strong enough for both regulators and funding bodies.

### Scenario 4: Protecting a Sensitive Coastal Habitat While Maintaining Production
The learner must be able to independently supervise production planning at a marine site located near sensitive benthic habitat, including Posidonia oceanica meadows. In this scenario, the learner reviews benthic monitoring data, sensor outputs, and historical site pressure to decide whether fallowing, cage repositioning, reduced stocking density, or extended recovery periods are necessary. The learner must balance production pressures with biodiversity protection obligations, communicate decisions to operational staff and management, and justify actions using evidence rather than opinion. The outcome should be a site management plan that protects habitat condition, maintains legal and financial credibility, and demonstrates that the farm can operate responsibly within a Taxonomy-aligned sustainability framework.
