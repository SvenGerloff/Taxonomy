## 1. Comprehensive Knowledge Requirements

### 1.1 What is the EU Taxonomy?

The EU Taxonomy is a common European classification system that helps define which economic activities can be considered environmentally sustainable. In simple terms, it is not a general “green label” for an entire company, and it is not a marketing slogan. It is a rule-based framework that asks a more specific question: **does a particular business activity make a real environmental contribution, and can that contribution be demonstrated with evidence?** The legal foundation is the **EU Taxonomy Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2020/852)**, adopted in 2020 as part of the European Green Deal and the EU’s wider effort to direct money toward sustainable investment. Before this regulation, many businesses, banks, and investors used different definitions of “sustainable,” which created confusion and allowed weak claims or “greenwashing.” The Taxonomy was created to reduce that confusion by giving everyone a shared reference point. For professionals in aquaculture, this matters because the framework increasingly influences how banks assess loans, how investors assess business plans, how large buyers evaluate suppliers, and how companies prepare sustainability reports.

To understand why this matters on the ground, it helps to separate what the Taxonomy does from what it does not do. It does **not** tell a fish farm manager exactly how to run cages, feed fish, clean tanks, or schedule harvesting. Instead, it creates a framework for checking whether the activity—such as fish farming, hatchery operation, processing, wastewater management, renewable energy installation, or side-stream reuse—supports one or more environmental objectives in a measurable way. For example, if a hatchery invests in a high-efficiency recirculating aquaculture system (RAS), the Taxonomy may be relevant because the company may want to show that the investment reduces water use, lowers pollution, improves energy performance, or supports climate objectives. A processor installing heat recovery systems, by-product valorisation lines, or improved refrigeration may also need to assess whether those investments align with Taxonomy criteria. So while the Taxonomy sounds like a high-level policy tool, it reaches all the way down to practical choices: pumps, aeration systems, feed strategies, waste handling, refrigeration upgrades, water testing routines, and records management.

The Taxonomy affects businesses in different ways depending on their size, financing structure, and market connections. Large companies and financial market actors are currently the most directly exposed, especially through sustainability disclosure obligations under related EU legislation. However, smaller operators often feel the impact indirectly. A farm may not be legally required to publish Taxonomy alignment data today, but its bank may ask for environmental performance information when reviewing a loan application. A seafood buyer may ask suppliers for evidence of resource efficiency, pollution controls, biodiversity safeguards, or climate measures. A parent company may require site managers to provide energy, water, feed, waste, and treatment records because it must prepare group-level sustainability reports. This is why professionals like Marco and Sofía need a practical understanding of the Taxonomy even if they do not work in finance or compliance. The first operational lesson is simple: if a business wants to claim that an aquaculture activity is sustainable in a way that matters to lenders, investors, and reporting systems, it must be able to provide documented evidence, not just verbal reassurance.

A key concept in the Taxonomy is how an activity becomes “green.” An economic activity is considered Taxonomy-aligned only if it meets several conditions together. First, it must make a **substantial contribution** to at least one of the six environmental objectives. Second, it must **do no significant harm (DNSH)** to the other objectives. Third, it must comply with **minimum social and governance safeguards**, such as responsible business conduct. Fourth, it must meet the relevant **technical screening criteria**, which are the detailed rules used to assess performance. For aquaculture, this means that a site cannot simply say, “We use less water than before, so we are sustainable.” It must ask: less water compared with what baseline? Is water quality still protected? Are discharges controlled? Is biodiversity protected? Are records complete? Is there evidence of compliance? In practice, the Taxonomy turns sustainability from a vague aspiration into a structured assessment. For a site manager or supervisor, that translates into a new way of looking at everyday operations: each operational improvement may also be a piece of evidence in a wider sustainability picture.

### 1.2 The Six Environmental Objectives

The EU Taxonomy is built around **six environmental objectives**, and these objectives are the backbone of the whole framework. They are: **(1) climate change mitigation, (2) climate change adaptation, (3) the sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources, (4) the transition to a circular economy, (5) pollution prevention and control, and (6) the protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems**. These objectives are established in the Taxonomy Regulation and then supported by delegated acts and technical screening criteria that define what counts as a meaningful contribution in practice. For learners new to the subject, the most important point is that the six objectives are not abstract environmental slogans. They are six practical lenses through which any activity—such as hatchery operation, marine cage farming, feed management, sludge handling, refrigeration, transport, or seafood processing—can be examined. The Taxonomy asks whether the activity clearly helps at least one of these objectives while not seriously damaging the others. This is especially relevant in aquaculture because operations interact closely with water, energy, biological systems, coastal space, waste streams, and local ecosystems.

The first objective, **climate change mitigation**, focuses on reducing or preventing greenhouse gas emissions. In aquaculture, this can relate to electricity use, diesel use in service vessels, oxygen generation, pumping, aeration, heating and cooling, transport, refrigeration, feed efficiency, and waste treatment. The second objective, **climate change adaptation**, is about preparing for the impacts of climate change rather than reducing emissions alone. This matters for farms and hatcheries facing rising water temperatures, marine heatwaves, storm damage, flooding, changing oxygen levels, salinity shifts, and disease pattern changes. The third objective, **water and marine resources**, is central to aquaculture because fish farming depends directly on water quality, water quantity, abstraction, circulation, filtration, discharge control, and ecosystem interaction. The fourth objective, **circular economy**, focuses on keeping materials and resources in use for as long as possible, reducing waste, and turning by-products into value. In aquaculture and seafood processing, this includes sludge reuse, fish trimmings valorisation, net repair, packaging redesign, and nutrient recovery.

The fifth objective, **pollution prevention and control**, deals with avoiding harmful emissions to air, water, and land, and reducing the use or release of hazardous substances. For aquaculture, this includes medicine management, antifoulants, disinfectants, cleaning agents, nutrient discharge, microplastics, fuel spills, and processing effluent. The sixth objective, **biodiversity and ecosystems**, is especially important in coastal and marine production systems because fish farms operate inside living ecosystems. This objective covers habitat protection, benthic integrity, site selection, protected species, invasive species management, and ecological restoration where needed. In practical terms, a farm near sensitive seagrass beds, shellfish habitats, migratory corridors, or Natura 2000 areas must pay close attention to this objective. A processing plant may seem less directly connected, but its sourcing choices, wastewater systems, and by-product disposal can still affect ecosystems.

For aquaculture professionals, understanding the six objectives means learning to see one operation through multiple environmental perspectives at once. A change that looks positive under one objective may create problems under another. For example, installing more powerful pumps may improve water circulation and fish welfare, but if the pumps are inefficient, they may increase energy consumption and worsen climate performance. Switching to a new cleaning chemical may improve hygiene, but if it creates higher aquatic toxicity in the discharge stream, it may conflict with pollution control and water protection goals. Increasing production density may improve output per square meter, but if it raises benthic loading or disease pressure, it may create biodiversity and pollution risks. This is why the Taxonomy uses a combined structure: substantial contribution plus DNSH. For managers and supervisors, the practical lesson is that sustainability decisions cannot be made in isolation. The six objectives provide a checklist for balanced operational judgement, where resource efficiency, resilience, compliance, and ecosystem protection all have to be considered together.

### 1.3 Aquaculture Alignment: How a Real Farm Assesses, Audits, and Proves Alignment

A real aquaculture business does not become Taxonomy-aligned by making a general statement such as “we operate responsibly” or “we care about the environment.” Alignment requires a structured process that connects site activities to legal criteria and operational evidence. The first step is to identify the **economic activity** under assessment. This is critical because the Taxonomy does not assess a company in one undivided block; it looks at specific activities. In an aquaculture context, different activities may exist within the same business: hatchery production, ongrowing in marine cages, land-based RAS grow-out, feed storage, wastewater treatment, processing, cold storage, transport, energy generation, or side-stream valorisation. Each activity may be treated differently. Once the activity is identified, the business must determine whether that activity is included in the scope of Taxonomy technical screening criteria and which environmental objective may be relevant. For example, a RAS investment might be examined under water efficiency, pollution control, climate mitigation, or circularity depending on what the project is designed to achieve. A manager’s first task is therefore mapping operations clearly: what do we do, where, with which equipment, using which inputs, and producing which outputs and wastes?

The second step is to test whether the activity makes a **substantial contribution** to at least one objective. This requires more than describing intentions. It means collecting data and comparing performance against relevant technical expectations. On a fish farm, this may involve energy bills, fuel logs, generator runtime, feed conversion ratio (FCR), mortality records, water use per kilogram of fish produced, effluent analysis, sludge capture rates, treatment system performance, maintenance logs, and biodiversity monitoring reports. In a hatchery, it may include UV sterilisation efficiency, recirculation rates, make-up water volumes, oxygen system performance, temperature control data, backup power resilience, and biofilter efficiency. In processing, it may involve kilograms of by-product valorised, wastewater chemical oxygen demand (COD), refrigeration energy intensity, packaging materials used, and recovery or recycling rates. Evidence matters because the Taxonomy is built for comparability and credibility. If a bank, auditor, buyer, or head office asks how a site contributes to environmental goals, the answer has to be supported by records, not memory.

The third step is checking **Do No Significant Harm (DNSH)** to the other five objectives. This is often where businesses discover that one good performance area does not automatically make an activity aligned. For example, suppose a farm installs solar panels and reduces grid electricity demand. That may help climate mitigation. But if the same farm has poor sludge management, inadequate discharge monitoring, repeated escapes, or weak benthic controls, then it may fail DNSH tests related to water protection, pollution, or biodiversity. A hatchery may have low water consumption thanks to recirculation, but if filter backwash is discharged without adequate treatment, or if chemical handling is poorly controlled, alignment may still be questionable. DNSH forces a more complete operational review: permits, incident logs, environmental impact assessments, emergency procedures, hazardous substance storage, waste contracts, site sensitivity, and maintenance of monitoring equipment all become relevant. For supervisors, DNSH is not extra theory; it is often the point where everyday site discipline—clean records, calibrated sensors, safe storage, planned maintenance, and accurate monitoring—becomes essential evidence.

The fourth step is formalising the proof through internal review, audit preparation, and reporting. A practical site-level alignment process usually includes: creating an activity inventory; identifying applicable objectives and criteria; assigning data owners; collecting baseline and current performance data; checking legal permits and environmental compliance history; reviewing operational risks under DNSH; documenting methods and assumptions; and preparing a simple evidence file. That evidence file might contain meter readings, sample reports, maintenance records, procurement documents, waste transfer notes, fish health protocols, farm maps, discharge permits, contractor agreements, and photographs of installed systems. An internal audit can then test whether records are complete, recent, and traceable. If a company wants to improve its future Taxonomy position, gap analysis is equally important. Maybe discharge data are collected but not linked to production volumes. Maybe biodiversity monitoring exists but lacks a standard reporting format. Maybe energy use is recorded monthly, but not by activity such as pumping, oxygenation, lighting, and refrigeration. The operational message for aquaculture professionals is clear: Taxonomy alignment is not mainly about legal interpretation. It is about turning everyday site management into an evidence-based system that shows how the farm performs, where risks exist, and what improvements are needed.

### 1.4 Climate Change Mitigation

Climate change mitigation in the Taxonomy context means reducing greenhouse gas emissions or preventing emissions that would otherwise occur. In aquaculture, emissions are not limited to fuel burned by boats or generators. They also come indirectly from electricity use, feed production, pumping, aeration, oxygen generation, heating, cooling, ice production, transportation, refrigeration, and waste handling. If a farm or processing site wants to contribute substantially to climate mitigation, it needs to understand where emissions come from across operations. A useful starting point is to divide emissions into three practical categories: **direct fuel use** (such as diesel in vessels, forklifts, or backup generators), **purchased energy** (electricity for pumps, blowers, RAS filtration, lighting, chillers, graders, and cold rooms), and **operational efficiency drivers** (especially feed conversion, mortality, and waste generation). For aquaculture professionals, the key insight is that climate performance is not only about installing renewable energy; it is also about improving how efficiently biological and mechanical systems operate every day. A farm with poor feed use, repeated stock losses, and inefficient pumping may carry a high climate burden even if it has one visible green technology on site.

One important mitigation pathway is the use of **solar and wind systems**, especially in sites with predictable energy loads or remote locations where diesel generation is expensive and emission-intensive. On land-based hatcheries and RAS units, rooftop solar photovoltaic panels can help power pumps, monitoring systems, lighting, office loads, and some daytime treatment equipment. On coastal sites, hybrid systems combining solar, small wind, and battery storage can reduce generator dependence for monitoring stations, feeding barges, security systems, and low-load support functions. These systems are not always capable of covering high continuous energy demand on their own, but they can still meaningfully reduce emissions if properly sized and integrated. The practical challenge is matching renewable generation to load profiles. A hatchery with steady 24-hour pumping demand may need battery storage or smart load management to benefit fully. Metrics that matter include kilowatt-hours generated on site, percentage of site electricity demand covered by renewables, diesel litres avoided, and estimated tonnes of CO2 equivalent reduced annually. For a manager, the lesson is that renewable systems should be treated as operational assets requiring maintenance, performance tracking, and backup planning—not as symbolic sustainability hardware.

Another major mitigation lever in aquaculture is **feed conversion optimization**, because feed is often one of the largest environmental burdens in fish production. Feed that is not converted into fish biomass becomes waste, cost, and unnecessary upstream emissions. Improving the **feed conversion ratio (FCR)** therefore supports both profitability and climate performance. Practical tools include camera-based feed monitoring, appetite sensors, automatic feeders with real-time adjustment, dissolved oxygen-linked feeding controls, pellet loss observations, and data analysis by biomass group and temperature band. Suppose a farm improves FCR from 1.50 to 1.30 in a production cycle of 1,000 tonnes of harvested fish. That difference represents 200 tonnes less feed used, which can significantly reduce embedded emissions from feed manufacturing, transport, and nutrient waste. Mortality reduction also matters because dead fish represent feed, energy, labour, and water already spent without productive output. In this way, stock management, fish health, feeding discipline, and biomass forecasting all become climate mitigation actions. This is especially important for operators like Marco, who already manage feeding and growth performance daily; the Taxonomy simply reframes these familiar decisions as part of environmental contribution.

Energy efficiency tools complete the picture. Many aquaculture sites can reduce emissions faster and more cheaply through efficiency measures than through major infrastructure replacement alone. Common examples include variable frequency drives (VFDs) on pumps, high-efficiency blowers, optimized aeration control, heat recovery from refrigeration systems, insulation of tanks and pipework, LED lighting, smart oxygen management, efficient grading schedules, and preventive maintenance that keeps motors and impellers operating properly. In processing plants, efficient compressors, cold room door control, ammonia or CO2 refrigeration upgrades, heat recovery from condensers, and real-time energy monitoring can significantly reduce energy intensity per tonne of product. Good mitigation practice also depends on measurement. Useful indicators include kWh per kilogram of fish produced, fuel litres per tonne harvested, kWh per cubic meter of recirculated water, FCR, mortality rate, and refrigeration energy per tonne processed. When these indicators are tracked consistently, site managers can identify where operational inefficiencies increase emissions. Climate mitigation under the Taxonomy is therefore not separate from production management—it is a more disciplined way of managing energy, feed, equipment, and biological performance together.

### 1.5 Climate Change Adaptation

Climate change adaptation differs from mitigation because it is about coping with the effects of climate change that are already happening or are expected to intensify. For aquaculture, adaptation is not a future issue; it is already affecting daily operations in many regions through marine heatwaves, warmer intake water, altered rainfall patterns, storm intensity, flooding, salinity changes, lower dissolved oxygen events, harmful algal blooms, and shifting disease pressure. In the EU Taxonomy, adaptation requires more than saying that a business is “aware” of climate risks. It expects that physical climate risks are identified, assessed, and managed in a structured way. That means a site must understand what climate hazards could affect production, infrastructure, water systems, logistics, and animal welfare, and then show how planning, investment, and operations reduce vulnerability. For site managers and supervisors, adaptation becomes practical when it is connected to questions they already face: What happens if intake water reaches critical temperature thresholds? Can cages withstand stronger storm conditions? What is the backup plan if flooding disables pumps or electrical panels? How quickly can fish be moved, oxygenated, or harvested if a severe weather event hits?

A central concept linked to adaptation is the **Do No Significant Harm (DNSH)** requirement. Even if an aquaculture investment contributes to one objective, it must not significantly harm climate adaptation or undermine resilience. For example, a new hatchery extension may improve production, but if it is built in a flood-prone area without resilient drainage, raised electrical systems, or emergency pumping capacity, it may fail the spirit of adaptation. Similarly, a processing upgrade that increases capacity but ignores cooling resilience during heatwaves may create future operational and food safety risks. In practice, adaptation work begins with a **climate risk and vulnerability assessment**. This should examine site-specific hazards such as high water temperature, low oxygen, sea-level rise, wave exposure, river flow variability, drought, storm surge, coastal erosion, and supply chain disruption. The assessment should then look at exposure and sensitivity: which assets are at risk, what would fail first, how severe would the consequences be, and what response capacity exists. For aquaculture businesses, this is not a desk exercise only. It should include maps, historical incident records, sensor data, maintenance history, fish health trends, and staff knowledge about previous climate-related disruptions.

Infrastructure resilience is a core adaptation area. In marine farms, this may involve stronger mooring systems, upgraded net materials, reinforced feeding barges, storm-ready service plans, and clear thresholds for stopping work and securing equipment. In hatcheries and RAS units, resilience may require backup generators sized for full life-support operation, duplicate pumps, protected oxygen supply, elevated electrical installations, flood barriers, emergency overflow systems, insulated pipework, and redundant alarms linked to remote monitoring. Processing plants may need stronger cooling systems, flood protection, cold chain contingency plans, and water storage capacity during drought or supply interruption. Adaptation also includes operational planning: flexible stocking densities during high-temperature periods, adjusted feeding protocols, harvest timing changes, revised transport windows, and health surveillance linked to environmental stressors. Practical resilience metrics can include number of hours of backup power available, percentage of critical systems with redundancy, days of oxygen reserve, infrastructure downtime due to extreme weather, and survival performance during temperature spikes or storm events. These are management indicators, not just engineering details.

**Temperature monitoring systems** are among the most practical adaptation tools because temperature affects metabolism, oxygen demand, pathogen development, feed intake, and mortality risk. Continuous monitoring with calibrated sensors at multiple depths or water circuit points helps operators detect stress early. In marine systems, this may include real-time profiling of temperature and dissolved oxygen through the water column. In hatcheries, it may involve automated tank-level sensors connected to alarms and historical trend software. Useful adaptation practice includes establishing threshold temperatures for reduced feeding, increased oxygenation, biomass redistribution, emergency harvest review, or temporary transfer. When temperature data are integrated with mortality, growth, and treatment records, farms can identify patterns and redesign management accordingly. The adaptation message of the Taxonomy is straightforward: a sustainable activity is not only one that emits less; it is also one that can remain environmentally sound and operationally safe under changing climate conditions. For aquaculture professionals, this means resilience must be built into infrastructure, routines, and decision-making—not left to emergency improvisation.

### 1.6 Water and Marine Resources

The sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources is one of the most directly relevant Taxonomy objectives for aquaculture because the sector is inseparable from water. Every production system—marine cages, ponds, flow-through hatcheries, shellfish operations, and recirculating systems—depends on water quality, water quantity, flow patterns, discharge management, and ecosystem interaction. Under this objective, the key question is whether the activity uses water responsibly and protects the surrounding aquatic environment. For learners without regulatory experience, the simplest way to understand this is through a balance: **what water is taken in, how efficiently it is used, what condition it is kept in during production, and what leaves the site afterward**. In marine farming, this includes how production interacts with currents, nutrient dispersion, benthic load, and sensitive habitats. In hatcheries and land-based systems, it includes intake, recirculation, filtration, treatment, and discharge. In processing plants, it includes wash water, cleaning systems, wastewater treatment, and final effluent quality. This objective matters because poor water stewardship can quickly create environmental, animal welfare, compliance, and reputational problems all at once.

A major technology linked to this objective is the **Recirculating Aquaculture System (RAS)**. RAS is often seen as a strong example of improved water efficiency because it treats and reuses most process water instead of continuously replacing it. A well-managed RAS can sharply reduce water withdrawal per kilogram of fish produced, but this does not automatically make it sustainable. The system must also control energy use, sludge removal, biofilter performance, off-flavour risks, gas balance, pathogen pressure, and discharge from purge events or backwash cycles. In practical terms, a hatchery supervisor like Sofía needs to understand both the promise and the discipline of RAS. Key performance metrics can include percentage recirculation rate, litres of make-up water per kilogram of biomass, ammonia and nitrite control, solids capture efficiency, pH stability, dissolved oxygen, carbon dioxide levels, sludge dryness, and final discharge quality. Water sustainability in RAS depends heavily on operations: if filters are poorly maintained, biofilters unstable, or alarms ignored, water savings may be offset by fish stress, treatment needs, or polluted discharges. So the Taxonomy lens encourages not only investment in advanced systems, but also consistent, documented performance.

Water treatment cycles are another core area. In hatcheries and processing plants, water does not simply “flow through”; it often passes through several treatment steps such as screening, settling, drum filtration, foam fractionation, biofiltration, UV disinfection, ozone treatment, degassing, oxygenation, and final effluent polishing. The practical importance of understanding treatment cycles is that each stage has a purpose, a maintenance need, and a measurable output. For example, solids removal systems reduce suspended particles that would otherwise increase oxygen demand and discharge load. Biofilters convert toxic ammonia into nitrate, protecting fish and stabilising system chemistry. UV systems reduce microbial load, but only if lamp intensity, flow rate, and quartz sleeve cleanliness are maintained. In processing environments, dissolved air flotation (DAF), screening, grease removal, pH correction, and biological wastewater treatment can reduce pollutant loads before discharge. Managers should know what each system removes, how performance is checked, and what happens if performance drops. Useful metrics include total suspended solids (TSS), biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen demand (COD), ammonia, nitrate, phosphate, flow rate, water reuse percentage, and compliance against permit limits.

**Discharge monitoring** is where water protection becomes visible and auditable. A site may claim good environmental practice, but without monitoring it cannot prove that nutrients, solids, chemicals, or organic matter are controlled. In marine cage systems, monitoring often includes benthic surveys, feed loss observations, current assessments, and nutrient loading estimates. In land-based systems and processing plants, it includes regular sampling of effluent quality and volume. Practical discharge management means knowing when samples are taken, by whom, using what method, under which permit conditions, and what trend is developing over time. It also means linking discharge results to production data. A high COD result means more when compared with biomass processed or fish biomass reared in the same period. Good management practice includes calibration of sensors, chain-of-custody for samples, incident reporting for exceedances, and corrective actions such as reduced loading, improved solids capture, cleaning of treatment units, or review of chemical use. Under the Taxonomy, sustainable use of water and marine resources is not only about using less water. It is about using water intelligently, protecting receiving environments, and being able to demonstrate that aquaculture production does not rely on hidden degradation of aquatic systems.

### 1.7 Circular Economy Transition

The circular economy objective asks whether an activity helps move away from the old linear model of **take, use, and discard** toward a system where materials, nutrients, energy, and components are kept in productive use for as long as possible. For aquaculture and seafood processing, this is highly relevant because the sector generates multiple side streams: sludge, mortalities, fish trimmings, blood water, shells, packaging waste, worn nets, ropes, pallets, feed bags, and process heat. Traditionally, many of these streams have been treated mainly as disposal problems. The circular economy perspective asks a different question: **what proportion of these outputs can be prevented, reused, repaired, remanufactured, recycled, or converted into valuable products?** This objective does not mean every waste stream must become a high-value product immediately. It means operations should be designed to reduce waste generation first and then improve how unavoidable residuals are captured and used. For practical learners, this is important because circularity often begins with simple operational improvements: better segregation, cleaner side-stream collection, less product loss, improved storage, and stronger tracking of material flows.

A strong aquaculture example is **sludge-to-biogas**. In hatcheries and RAS systems, solids captured from drum filters, settlers, and sludge cones contain organic matter and nutrients. If this sludge is simply diluted and discharged, it becomes a pollution issue and a lost resource. If it is thickened, stabilised, and sent to anaerobic digestion, it can contribute to biogas production, generating renewable energy and digestate that may have agricultural value if handled under the right standards. The practical challenge lies in water content, contamination, logistics, and consistency of supply. Wet sludge is expensive to transport, so dewatering equipment such as screw presses, geotextile dewatering bags, or mechanical thickeners may be needed. For a site manager, the circular economy lesson is operational: sludge must be collected in a way that preserves its value. If it is mixed with unsuitable chemicals, excessive debris, or uncontrolled wash water, its recovery options become weaker. Metrics include tonnes of sludge captured, dry matter percentage, percentage sent to recovery rather than disposal, cubic meters of biogas generated, and tonnes of CO2 equivalent avoided through energy substitution.

Another important circular pathway is creating **fertilizer by-products** from nutrient-rich aquaculture residuals. Sludge and some processing residues contain nitrogen, phosphorus, and organic matter that can support soil improvement or nutrient recycling if regulatory and hygiene conditions are met. This can be particularly relevant in integrated local systems where aquaculture is linked with agriculture or soil restoration. However, circular use is only credible when quality is controlled. The material must be characterised for moisture, nutrient content, heavy metals, pathogens, and contaminants. Storage and transport also matter because nutrient loss, odour, or runoff can undermine the environmental benefit. In some cases, fish processing by-products may also enter other value chains, such as fish meal, fish oil, pet food, collagen, enzyme extraction, or specialised feed ingredients. The circular economy is therefore not limited to farm waste; it includes the whole seafood chain. A processing supervisor like Tomasz may play a major role by improving trim segregation, cold storage of side streams, and documentation of destination routes so that more material goes to valorisation and less to low-value disposal.

**Packaging waste reduction** is another highly visible circular measure, especially in hatcheries and processing plants. Feed bags, liners, polystyrene boxes, plastic straps, shrink wrap, and single-use packaging are common operational realities. A circular approach examines whether packaging can be reduced at source, redesigned, reused, or switched to materials with better recovery potential. Examples include bulk feed delivery instead of bagged feed, reusable fish transport containers, returnable pallets, recyclable mono-material packaging, or improved packaging specifications that reduce damage and product loss. Good circular management also requires segregation systems that work for staff under real production conditions. If recyclables are mixed with organic waste, water, or chemicals, recovery becomes difficult. Useful metrics include kilograms of packaging per tonne of product, percentage of reusable packaging units, recycling rate by material type, by-product recovery rate, and waste disposal cost per tonne produced. In Taxonomy terms, circular economy transition is not an abstract innovation slogan. It is a practical redesign of how materials move through the operation so that value is kept in the system and waste becomes the last option rather than the first.

### 1.8 Pollution Prevention and Control

Pollution prevention and control focuses on avoiding harmful releases to water, air, and land, and reducing dependence on hazardous substances where safer alternatives or tighter controls are possible. In aquaculture, pollution can come from many sources beyond obvious waste discharge. Uneaten feed, faeces, suspended solids, disinfectants, antifoulants, fuel spills, lubricants, treatment chemicals, nutrient loading, cleaning agents, microplastics from gear, and medicine residues can all affect environmental quality. For professionals working on farms or in hatcheries, pollution prevention is often already part of routine good practice, even if it has not been described in formal environmental terms. Keeping fuel tanks secure, storing chemicals correctly, avoiding feed wastage, maintaining filters, and controlling washdown water are all pollution prevention actions. The Taxonomy perspective strengthens this by asking for systematic control and evidence. It is not enough that pollution incidents are “usually avoided.” The operation should be designed and managed so that pollution risks are identified, prevented where possible, monitored where necessary, and corrected quickly if something goes wrong.

One practical example is the move toward **zero-runoff systems** or low-discharge design. In land-based aquaculture, zero runoff rarely means literally nothing ever leaves the site; rather, it means that process water, stormwater, sludge liquor, and cleaning flows are contained, treated, and reused or discharged under tightly controlled conditions. This can involve separate drainage systems for clean rainwater and contaminated process water, bunded chemical storage, collection pits, treatment tanks, biofilters, wetlands, and emergency shut-off arrangements. In hatcheries, this matters because a great deal of contamination risk comes from mixing streams that should stay separate. If chemical wash water enters the same route as biological solids or if backwash bypasses treatment, the site may undermine both water protection and pollution control. In processing plants, runoff control is especially important around unloading areas, blood water handling, by-product storage, and cleaning operations. Useful metrics include number of uncontrolled discharge incidents, percentage of drainage lines mapped, compliance with effluent permit limits, and response time to spills or containment failures. The principle is simple: pollution control starts with engineering and housekeeping, not only with end-of-pipe treatment.

**Chemical inventory management** is another essential pollution prevention tool. Aquaculture sites often use disinfectants, detergents, pH adjustment chemicals, oxygen system materials, antifoulants, lubricants, fuels, veterinary products, and laboratory reagents. Problems arise when these substances are poorly labelled, stored without secondary containment, kept beyond expiry, mixed inappropriately, or used without clear dosing and disposal procedures. A good chemical inventory should identify what is on site, where it is stored, in what quantity, for what purpose, with which safety information, and who is responsible. In practical supervisory terms, this means every chemical should have a known owner, an approved storage location, and a procedure for normal use and emergency response. Secondary containment, spill kits, locked storage, incompatible substance separation, and staff training are basic but powerful controls. From a Taxonomy viewpoint, this supports DNSH by reducing the likelihood that one operational area—such as sanitation or net maintenance—creates significant environmental harm elsewhere. A manager should also be able to show records of deliveries, usage, disposal, and incidents, because traceability is part of credible pollution control.

A particularly important area in aquaculture is reducing the risk of pollution linked to **antimicrobial resistance (AMR)** and drug use. Medicines may be necessary in some situations, but overuse, poor targeting, or weak preventive health management can create environmental and public concern. Reducing AMR-related risk does not mean refusing treatment when fish welfare requires it; it means strengthening disease prevention so that routine dependence on antimicrobials becomes less likely. Good practice includes vaccination programs where appropriate, biosecurity discipline, quarantine procedures, water quality control, lower stress through better stocking and handling, diagnostic testing before treatment, veterinary oversight, and post-treatment review. Feed management also matters because stress and poor water quality can increase disease susceptibility. Monitoring indicators can include total antimicrobial use by biomass, number of treatment events per production cycle, mortality trends, pathogen surveillance results, and biosecurity non-conformance reports. Under the Taxonomy, pollution prevention and control is ultimately about operational maturity: a sustainable site is one where pollution is not treated as an occasional accident but as a managed risk that is reduced through design, training, monitoring, and continuous improvement.

### 1.9 Biodiversity and Ecosystem Protection

The protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems is one of the most sensitive and important objectives for aquaculture because the sector often operates directly inside natural habitats or in close connection with them. Biodiversity includes the variety of species, habitats, and ecological relationships in a given area. Ecosystem protection means maintaining the health and function of those systems rather than reducing them to simple production space. For aquaculture professionals, this objective becomes practical when translated into site-based questions: Is the farm located near sensitive habitats? Are benthic conditions beneath and around cages being maintained? Is there evidence of harmful enrichment? Could escapes affect wild populations? Are bird, mammal, or other wildlife interactions managed responsibly? Are intake and discharge systems affecting local ecosystems? The Taxonomy’s concern is not whether aquaculture can exist at all, but whether it is operated in a way that avoids significant ecological damage and, where possible, contributes to better ecosystem outcomes. This is especially relevant in coastal Europe, where aquaculture may overlap with protected areas, nursery grounds, seagrass meadows, shellfish beds, or migratory routes.

One practical biodiversity tool is the use of **fallowing schedules**. Fallowing means leaving a production site or cage area unstocked for a planned period so the local environment can recover and disease pressure can be reduced. In marine cage systems, coordinated fallowing between production cycles can help break pathogen cycles, reduce benthic organic loading, and allow sediment conditions to improve. Fallowing is most effective when linked to site carrying capacity, benthic monitoring, and biomass planning rather than being treated as a calendar formality. For a site manager, this means understanding that recovery time should match the environmental pressure created by the previous cycle. If high feed loads, weak currents, or elevated organic deposition occurred, longer or more carefully timed fallow periods may be needed. Metrics can include duration of fallowing, benthic recovery indicators before restocking, macrofauna diversity, sulphide levels, redox potential, and visual sediment scoring where used. Fallowing is a good example of how operational scheduling and biodiversity protection can support each other rather than conflict. A healthier benthic environment also helps social licence, compliance, and long-term site productivity.

Modern biodiversity management increasingly uses **benthic sensor deployment** and other monitoring technologies. Traditional benthic assessments often rely on periodic sampling, diver inspections, grab samples, or laboratory analysis of sediments and fauna. These remain important, but real-time or more frequent monitoring tools can improve responsiveness. Sensors may track dissolved oxygen near the seabed, turbidity, current speed, temperature, or proxies for deposition. Camera systems, remote operated vehicles (ROVs), and acoustic tools can support visual checks of substrate conditions, net integrity, and habitat interaction. In land-based systems, biodiversity concerns may focus more on discharge pathways, wetland interfaces, nearby streams, or estuarine receiving waters. The practical value of monitoring technology is not just data collection; it is better management timing. If benthic indicators show worsening trends, feeding strategies, biomass levels, site rotation, or harvest timing may need review. Managers should understand the limits of each monitoring method, because sensor data without interpretation can be misleading. Useful biodiversity oversight combines technology, ecological expertise, permit requirements, and site experience. Under a Taxonomy lens, the stronger and more traceable the monitoring system, the easier it is to demonstrate that the activity is not significantly harming ecosystems.

The objective also includes habitat restoration where relevant, and one example often discussed in Mediterranean contexts is **Posidonia oceanica restoration metrics**. Posidonia oceanica is a seagrass species of high ecological importance, providing nursery habitat, carbon storage, and coastal protection. It is also sensitive to disturbance, shading, sedimentation, and water quality changes. If aquaculture takes place near such habitats, monitoring and avoidance are critical. Restoration may involve transplant survival studies, shoot density counts, area restored, sediment stability indicators, and long-term habitat condition monitoring. Even where a specific farm is not involved directly in restoration, the idea is useful because it shows how biodiversity performance can be measured in concrete ways. Similar principles apply to other habitats: shellfish reef condition, macroalgal cover, bird nesting disturbance, or stream invertebrate diversity downstream of discharges. For aquaculture professionals, the main lesson is that biodiversity protection is not just about avoiding visible damage. It is about understanding ecological sensitivity, planning operations around it, and using measurable indicators to show that production and ecosystem health are being managed together. In Taxonomy terms, biodiversity is not the final box to tick after everything else; it is one of the core conditions that determines whether an activity can credibly be described as sustainable.

## 2. Practical and Cognitive Skills

After completing this module, the learner should be able to:

- Explain in plain language what the EU Taxonomy is and why it matters to aquaculture businesses, banks, investors, buyers, and reporting systems.
- Distinguish between a company-level sustainability claim and a Taxonomy assessment of a specific economic activity.
- Identify the legal foundation of the Taxonomy, including the role of Regulation (EU) 2020/852 and technical screening criteria.
- Name and describe the six environmental objectives without relying on policy jargon.
- Translate each environmental objective into site-level aquaculture examples relevant to farms, hatcheries, and processing facilities.
- Recognize the difference between **substantial contribution** and **Do No Significant Harm (DNSH)**.
- Explain why an activity can perform well in one environmental area but still fail overall alignment if it harms another objective.
- Map the main economic activities within an aquaculture business, such as hatchery operation, grow-out, RAS production, processing, waste handling, or energy generation.
- Carry out a basic Taxonomy relevance screening for a specific aquaculture activity.
- Gather and organize operational evidence needed to support a sustainability assessment.
- Build a simple evidence file containing permits, monitoring data, maintenance logs, sampling records, waste transfer documentation, and performance metrics.
- Identify which site records are most important for demonstrating environmental performance.
- Conduct a basic gap analysis to determine what evidence is missing for a Taxonomy alignment review.
- Link common aquaculture metrics to environmental objectives, including:
  - feed conversion ratio (FCR)
  - mortality rate
  - kWh per kg produced
  - litres of fuel per tonne harvested
  - water use per kg biomass
  - sludge capture rate
  - effluent quality indicators such as BOD, COD, TSS, ammonia, nitrate, and phosphate
- Interpret how feed efficiency affects both profitability and climate change mitigation.
- Assess whether renewable energy options such as solar or wind are technically relevant to a site’s energy profile.
- Identify practical energy efficiency opportunities, including VFDs, efficient blowers, pump optimization, heat recovery, insulation, and smart monitoring.
- Read basic energy consumption data and identify unusual patterns or avoidable losses.
- Carry out a simple climate risk and vulnerability review for a farm, hatchery, or processing plant.
- Identify adaptation risks linked to heatwaves, storms, flooding, low oxygen events, salinity change, and supply chain disruption.
- Recommend appropriate resilience measures such as backup power, redundant pumps, raised electrical systems, reinforced moorings, and emergency oxygen reserves.
- Use temperature and dissolved oxygen trends to support operational adaptation decisions.
- Recognize when current infrastructure may be vulnerable to future climate conditions.
- Explain how RAS supports water efficiency and what operational controls are required to make it environmentally credible.
- Describe key water treatment steps, including solids removal, biofiltration, UV treatment, degassing, oxygenation, and effluent polishing.
- Interpret discharge monitoring reports and compare results against permit conditions or internal thresholds.
- Link water protection performance to production volume and operational load.
- Identify practical circular economy opportunities in aquaculture, including:
  - sludge-to-biogas
  - fertilizer recovery
  - fish by-product valorisation
  - packaging reduction and redesign
  - improved segregation of waste streams
- Evaluate whether a side-stream is being lost as waste or managed as a potential resource.
- Improve waste segregation practices so that recycling and recovery are more feasible.
- Identify pollution risks from feed loss, cleaning agents, fuels, medicines, antifoulants, microplastics, and runoff.
- Review a chemical inventory for completeness, storage risk, traceability, and disposal control.
- Check whether bunding, spill response, incompatible storage separation, and labelling are adequate.
- Explain the operational importance of responsible antimicrobial use and AMR risk reduction.
- Identify practical measures to reduce treatment dependence through better biosecurity, water quality control, and fish health planning.
- Interpret biodiversity-related operational controls such as site rotation, fallowing, benthic monitoring, and habitat buffer zones.
- Understand the role of benthic sensors, ROVs, and ecological surveys in monitoring environmental impact.
- Recognize when a farm may be operating near sensitive habitat and what additional caution is required.
- Use simple ecological indicators to support management decisions on restocking, biomass loading, and site recovery.
- Communicate sustainability requirements clearly to operational teams who may resist “extra paperwork.”
- Convert abstract sustainability expectations into practical job instructions for technicians and operators.
- Prepare for internal or external audit questions by linking operational routines to documented evidence.
- Escalate environmental risks to senior management with clear factual support.
- Support decision-making on future investments by comparing environmental benefit, operational feasibility, and evidence requirements.
- Contribute to continuous improvement plans that strengthen future Taxonomy alignment.

## 3. Management and Supervisory Autonomy Outcomes

### Scenario 1: Bank Financing Request for a Farm Upgrade
The learner can independently manage a situation in which the company seeks financing for a hatchery or farm upgrade and the bank requests evidence that the project supports sustainability objectives. The learner must be able to identify which site activity is being financed, gather the relevant operational data, explain which Taxonomy objective is most relevant, and assemble practical evidence such as energy records, water use data, discharge monitoring results, equipment specifications, and compliance documents. They must also recognize where evidence is weak and propose realistic corrective actions before submission.

### Scenario 2: Climate Stress Event Affecting Production
The learner can independently respond when a site experiences abnormal temperatures, low oxygen, or storm-related disruption that threatens stock welfare and operational continuity. They must be able to interpret monitoring data, activate adaptation measures such as backup systems or revised feeding plans, protect staff and stock, document the event, and review whether infrastructure and procedures are still adequate under changing climate conditions. They should also be able to brief management on what happened, what environmental risks arose, and what resilience upgrades are now justified.

### Scenario 3: Environmental Audit Finds Gaps in Water, Waste, or Pollution Controls
The learner can independently handle an internal or external audit finding showing incomplete discharge records, weak chemical inventory management, poor sludge tracking, or unclear waste segregation. They must be able to investigate root causes, reorganize documentation, assign responsibilities, correct operational weaknesses, and create a practical action plan that staff can follow. This includes balancing production pressure with environmental control, ensuring records become routine rather than one-off, and preventing repeat non-conformities.

### Scenario 4: Operational Decision with Conflicting Sustainability Impacts
The learner can independently evaluate a real-world decision where one improvement may help one environmental objective but create risk for another. For example, installing new high-capacity pumps may improve fish welfare and water quality but increase energy use; increasing biomass may improve output efficiency but worsen benthic pressure; switching packaging may reduce plastic weight but weaken recyclability. The learner must be able to compare the trade-offs, apply the logic of substantial contribution and DNSH, consult the right data, and recommend a balanced decision that protects operations while strengthening overall sustainability performance.
