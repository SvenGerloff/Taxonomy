## 1. Comprehensive Knowledge Requirements

### 1.1 What is the EU Taxonomy?

The EU Taxonomy is the European Union’s common classification system for environmentally sustainable economic activities. In simple terms, it is a rulebook that helps businesses, banks, investors, and public authorities decide which investments can genuinely be called “green.” It was formally established through Regulation (EU) 2020/852, usually called the Taxonomy Regulation, adopted in 2020 as part of the European Green Deal and the EU’s wider Sustainable Finance agenda. Before the Taxonomy, many businesses used sustainability claims loosely, and investors often struggled to compare one project with another. One aquaculture company might say its farm is sustainable because it uses less water, while another claims sustainability because it reduced diesel use. The Taxonomy was created to bring order to this situation by introducing a shared language, technical criteria, and a legal basis for judging environmental performance. It does not replace environmental law, licensing, or veterinary controls. Instead, it sits above them as a framework for defining whether an activity makes a substantial environmental contribution and avoids causing serious harm elsewhere.

For aquaculture professionals, the important point is that the EU Taxonomy is not an abstract Brussels concept with no impact on day-to-day farm work. It increasingly affects access to finance, investor confidence, grant competitiveness, and long-term business planning. If a farm wants to apply for EMFAF funding, partner in a LIFE project, attract Horizon Europe innovation support, or negotiate an EIB green loan, the ability to demonstrate alignment with Taxonomy principles can become a major advantage. This does not mean every fish farmer must become a lawyer. It means farm managers need to understand how practical operational decisions—energy systems, water recirculation, sludge handling, seabed monitoring, antibiotic management, infrastructure resilience—can be translated into evidence that supports a sustainability claim. A farm that installs a solar-wind hybrid system, recovers sludge into biogas, and proves low discharge through monitoring is not only improving operations; it is building a documented case that its activities contribute to recognized EU environmental objectives.

The Taxonomy determines whether an economic activity is “taxonomy-aligned” by applying several tests. First, the activity must be covered by the Taxonomy’s technical screening criteria or fit within a recognized activity category. Second, it must make a substantial contribution to at least one of the six environmental objectives. Third, it must Do No Significant Harm (DNSH) to the other five objectives. Fourth, it must meet minimum safeguards, such as basic labour, human rights, and governance standards. This structure is very important for aquaculture because a farm cannot claim to be green only because it improved one area. For example, a hatchery may save water through a Recirculating Aquaculture System (RAS), but if it causes serious pollution through poorly managed sludge or damages seabed biodiversity through discharge, it may fail alignment. The Taxonomy therefore pushes businesses toward balanced performance. It rewards integrated thinking, not isolated green actions. This is especially relevant in aquaculture, where environmental systems are connected: feed affects water quality, water quality affects health, health affects medicine use, medicine use affects marine ecosystems, and energy systems affect costs and emissions.

In operational terms, the EU Taxonomy changes the question from “Are we doing something sustainable?” to “Can we prove, with data and evidence, that this farm activity makes a measurable environmental contribution without creating major harm elsewhere?” That shift matters. It means farms need records, monitoring, maintenance logs, energy data, water quality reports, benthic surveys, and waste recovery figures. It also means managers must become better at linking everyday practices to environmental outcomes. Replacing a diesel generator with renewables is not just an engineering upgrade; it is a mitigation measure. Installing stronger moorings for storm resilience is not just risk management; it can support adaptation. Operating a closed-loop sludge treatment system is not just housekeeping; it may support circular economy and pollution prevention objectives. In this sense, the EU Taxonomy is both a compliance tool and a management tool. It helps farms organize sustainability efforts in a way that is intelligible to funders, regulators, and business partners. For learners at EQF Level 5, the goal is not memorizing legal articles but being able to interpret this framework and apply it confidently in a working aquaculture facility.

### 1.2 The Six Environmental Objectives

The EU Taxonomy is built around six environmental objectives defined in the Taxonomy Regulation. These are: climate change mitigation; climate change adaptation; sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources; transition to a circular economy; pollution prevention and control; and protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems. These objectives are the core structure of the framework. They provide the categories under which an activity can show a “substantial contribution.” For aquaculture, this matters because the sector interacts directly with energy, water, nutrients, marine environments, and local ecosystems. Unlike office-based sectors, aquaculture operations leave visible physical traces in the environment and depend on environmental quality for business success. The six objectives therefore map naturally onto how a farm is designed, operated, monitored, and improved. In the CircuTech module, these objectives are translated into operational zones so learners can understand them through the physical spaces of a real facility rather than through abstract legal language.

The first two objectives, climate change mitigation and climate change adaptation, are linked but distinct. Mitigation is about reducing or avoiding greenhouse gas emissions. In aquaculture, this includes replacing diesel-powered systems with solar-wind hybrids, improving pump efficiency, reducing feed waste, optimizing aeration, and lowering transport emissions. Adaptation, on the other hand, is about preparing the farm for climate impacts that are already happening or are expected to happen, such as marine heatwaves, stronger storms, salinity shifts, water scarcity, or flooding. Legally, the Taxonomy supports both objectives because the EU recognizes that sustainability means both cutting emissions and building resilience. A farm that invests in floating cage stability, emergency oxygen backup, temperature monitoring, and heat stress response plans is acting under adaptation principles. In practice, aquaculture managers must understand that a low-carbon farm is not automatically a climate-resilient farm. Both dimensions need attention and evidence.

The third and fourth objectives—sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources, and transition to a circular economy—are especially relevant to hatcheries and waste processing areas. Water and marine resources focus on the responsible management of freshwater, seawater, discharge, aquatic ecosystems, and the quality of receiving waters. In aquaculture, this includes technologies such as RAS, mechanical filtration, biofiltration, UV or ozone treatment, oxygen management, and discharge monitoring for nitrogen, phosphorus, suspended solids, and biological oxygen demand. The circular economy objective goes beyond waste reduction. It asks whether resources are kept in use for as long as possible and whether outputs traditionally treated as waste—such as sludge, mortalities, packaging, nets, or heat—can be recovered, reused, repaired, recycled, or converted into value. A sludge-to-biogas unit is a strong example because it transforms a disposal problem into energy and fertilizer products. This objective encourages managers to stop seeing waste streams as unavoidable costs and start managing them as recoverable materials.

The fifth and sixth objectives—pollution prevention and control, and biodiversity and ecosystem protection—deal with impacts that are often under public and regulatory scrutiny in aquaculture. Pollution prevention includes reducing nutrient runoff, preventing chemical leaks, minimizing disinfectant overuse, controlling medicine residues, and limiting antimicrobial resistance risks. It requires practical systems: bunded chemical storage, stock control, emergency spill procedures, zero-runoff infrastructure, and evidence-based veterinary use. Biodiversity and ecosystems focus on habitat condition, species interactions, seabed integrity, and restoration where damage has occurred. In coastal and marine aquaculture, this may involve benthic monitoring under cages, fallowing schedules, controls on organic loading, escape prevention, and restoration support for habitats such as Posidonia oceanica meadows. Legally, these objectives are tied to wider EU environmental law, including marine, water, and habitat protection frameworks. Operationally, they force a whole-farm perspective. A site cannot be considered sustainable only because it is efficient or profitable if it degrades the surrounding ecosystem. For aquaculture professionals, the six objectives are best understood as a complete environmental map of the farm: energy, resilience, water, materials, emissions, and ecosystems. The task is to understand how each zone of the facility contributes to one or more of these objectives and how the evidence is collected to prove it.

### 1.3 Aquaculture Alignment — How a Real Farm Assesses, Audits, and Proves Alignment

Aquaculture alignment with the EU Taxonomy begins with identifying what the farm actually does as an economic activity and then matching those activities to the relevant environmental objectives. A real farm should not begin with paperwork; it should begin with an operational map. This means listing the main processes and areas of the business: energy generation and consumption, pumping and aeration, hatchery operations, water intake and discharge, feed storage and feeding systems, fish health management, sludge handling, waste disposal, transport, seabed impacts, and environmental monitoring. In the CircuTech framework, this is simplified through Zones A to E. Zone A covers energy and infrastructure, Zone B the hatchery and water management, Zone C waste processing, Zone D the coastal seabed, and Zone E the final synthesis and action plan. This zoning method helps learners see that Taxonomy alignment is not one single audit but a structured review of how each part of the farm contributes to sustainability. The first practical step is therefore to establish a baseline: how much electricity and fuel are used per tonne of production, how much water is consumed, what percentage is recirculated, what volume of sludge is generated, what is discharged, what chemicals are stored, and what biodiversity monitoring exists.

The second stage is gap assessment against the Taxonomy logic: substantial contribution, DNSH, and minimum safeguards. For each operational area, the farm asks three questions. First, what environmental objective does this area support? Second, what evidence proves substantial contribution? Third, does this activity risk causing serious harm to the other objectives? For example, a farm may claim substantial contribution to climate mitigation because it replaced diesel with a solar-wind hybrid and battery storage. Evidence may include fuel consumption records before and after installation, electricity generation logs, maintenance records, and emission reduction estimates in tonnes of CO2 equivalent. However, DNSH questions must also be asked. Was the installation placed in a way that damages sensitive habitat? Are waste batteries managed safely? Does the infrastructure withstand severe weather, or does it create adaptation vulnerabilities? This disciplined review prevents narrow thinking. The same applies to RAS systems: recirculating 95% of water is a strong contribution to water protection, but the farm must still manage sludge, chemical use, and energy consumption properly. Alignment therefore requires cross-checking, not box-ticking.

The third stage is documentation and audit readiness. Many farms already perform sustainable practices but fail to capture evidence in a credible and organized way. Taxonomy alignment depends heavily on traceability. Managers should build an evidence file or digital compliance register containing permits, monitoring records, standard operating procedures, calibration logs, laboratory analyses, maintenance records, invoices for installed technologies, incident reports, staff training records, and performance dashboards. Typical metrics include kilowatt-hours per kilogram of fish produced, litres of diesel avoided, percentage of renewable energy used on site, feed conversion ratio, percentage of water recirculated, nitrogen and phosphorus concentrations in discharge, kilograms of sludge recovered, cubic metres of biogas produced, number of medicine treatments per cycle, benthic condition scores, and biodiversity recovery indicators. A good farm audit process also assigns responsibility: who records water quality, who verifies waste volumes, who signs off maintenance, who updates antibiotic use logs, and who prepares the investor-ready summary. At EQF Level 5, learners must be able to supervise this process, not simply participate in it. They need to know how to turn scattered operational data into structured sustainability evidence.

The final stage is using the alignment assessment to access funding and improve business strategy. Once compliance is assessed and evidence is assembled, the farm can prepare applications for EMFAF grants, LIFE environmental projects, Horizon Europe demonstration or innovation actions, or EIB green financing. The application pathway is practical and usually follows a clear logic: assess compliance, document evidence, submit application, undergo expert verification, and receive funding if approved. But alignment should not be treated only as an application exercise. It should also drive internal planning. If the audit shows the farm performs strongly on water recirculation but weakly on biodiversity evidence, the manager can plan investments in benthic sensors or fallowing improvements. If renewable energy reduces emissions but creates intermittent supply issues, battery capacity or demand management may need review. In this way, Taxonomy alignment becomes a management cycle: assess, improve, document, verify, finance, and reinvest. For aquaculture professionals, the value of this approach is that it turns sustainability from a vague claim into a set of operational decisions linked to performance, compliance, and finance. A farm that can prove alignment is not only greener on paper; it is often more efficient, more resilient, and more attractive to markets and lenders.

### 1.4 Climate Change Mitigation — Solar-Wind Systems, Feed Conversion Optimization, Energy Efficiency Tools

Climate change mitigation in aquaculture means reducing the greenhouse gas emissions associated with farm operations. On a practical level, emissions in aquaculture usually come from electricity consumption, diesel use, transport, feed production, pumping, aeration, oxygen generation, heating or cooling, and waste decomposition. For many farms, especially remote coastal facilities, diesel generators are a major source of both emissions and operating cost. A mitigation strategy begins by identifying the biggest emission sources and then replacing, reducing, or optimizing them. In Zone A of the CircuTech model, a key example is replacing diesel generators with a solar-wind hybrid energy system. This combination is often more suitable than solar alone in coastal areas because wind resources can be strong during periods when sunlight is lower. A hybrid setup may include photovoltaic panels, small wind turbines, inverters, battery storage, and smart controllers that manage energy flows between generation, storage, and demand. For a farm manager, the key metrics are annual diesel litres displaced, percentage of on-site renewable energy, kilowatt-hours generated from renewables, downtime avoided, and estimated tonnes of CO2 equivalent reduced. These numbers are the bridge between technical investment and Taxonomy evidence.

Feed conversion optimization is another major mitigation area that is sometimes underestimated because it is not always seen as an “energy project.” Yet feed is one of the largest environmental burdens in aquaculture, both in financial and carbon terms. A poor feed conversion ratio (FCR) means more feed is required to produce each kilogram of fish, increasing upstream emissions from feed manufacture and transport, while also increasing nutrient loading in the water. Improving FCR through better feed quality, better feed timing, biomass estimation, and sensor-based feeding can significantly lower the farm’s climate impact. Technologies such as underwater cameras, acoustic feeding sensors, pellet detection systems, and AI-assisted feeding platforms help reduce overfeeding by adjusting delivery to fish behaviour in real time. If a farm improves its FCR from 1.8 to 1.4, the climate and cost benefits can be substantial over a production cycle. Managers should track feed input per production batch, feed wastage observations, fish growth performance, and mortality-adjusted FCR. This is a strong example of how operational excellence and climate mitigation often support each other rather than compete.

Energy efficiency tools are the third pillar of mitigation. Before investing in new generation systems, a farm should make sure it is not wasting energy through inefficient equipment or poor scheduling. Pumps, blowers, oxygen systems, heating systems, and lighting should be reviewed through an energy audit. Variable frequency drives (VFDs) can reduce electricity use by matching pump speed to actual demand rather than running motors continuously at full load. High-efficiency motors, insulated pipework, improved aeration controls, heat recovery systems, and automated shutoff routines can all lower energy intensity. In hatcheries and RAS facilities, energy intensity is often measured in kilowatt-hours per cubic metre of water treated or per kilogram of fish produced. Monitoring systems that display live power consumption can help supervisors identify abnormal energy spikes caused by fouled filters, failing motors, blocked pipes, or incorrect operating settings. Energy mitigation therefore requires both technology and management discipline. It is not enough to install better equipment; staff must be trained to use it correctly, maintain it regularly, and interpret performance data.

From a Taxonomy perspective, climate mitigation actions must be measurable, documented, and integrated with DNSH considerations. A renewable installation should not create new environmental harm, and an efficiency measure should not compromise animal welfare or system reliability. For example, reducing aeration to save energy is unacceptable if dissolved oxygen falls below safe levels. Similarly, installing wind infrastructure in a sensitive area without ecological assessment could undermine biodiversity objectives. The best mitigation plans are therefore balanced plans. A farm might combine a solar-wind hybrid system, smart battery management, VFD-controlled pumps, efficient oxygenation, optimized feeding, and route planning for feed delivery and harvest logistics. The manager then documents baseline energy use, post-upgrade performance, maintenance routines, and emissions reductions. This package not only improves environmental performance but strengthens applications for EMFAF, LIFE, or green finance. In practical terms, climate mitigation should be understood not as one project but as an operational culture: every litre of fuel, every wasted pellet, every unnecessary pump hour, and every inefficient process is a potential source of avoidable emissions and avoidable cost.

### 1.5 Climate Change Adaptation — DNSH Criteria, Infrastructure Resilience, Temperature Monitoring Systems

Climate change adaptation in aquaculture means preparing the farm to continue operating safely and productively under changing environmental conditions. Unlike mitigation, which focuses on reducing emissions, adaptation focuses on risks such as stronger storms, heatwaves, water scarcity, salinity changes, harmful algal blooms, oxygen depletion events, coastal erosion, and infrastructure stress. Aquaculture is highly exposed because it depends directly on environmental stability. Fish and shellfish are sensitive to temperature and oxygen fluctuations, cages and moorings are exposed to extreme weather, and water systems can become unreliable during droughts or heavy rainfall. Under the EU Taxonomy, adaptation is recognized as a distinct environmental objective, and it is highly relevant to DNSH thinking as well. A farm cannot claim to be sustainable if its systems are likely to fail under foreseeable climate conditions, causing escapes, mortalities, pollution incidents, or financial collapse. Adaptation therefore starts with a climate risk and vulnerability assessment: what hazards are increasing, how exposed is the site, which assets are vulnerable, and what operational consequences could follow?

In Zone A, infrastructure resilience is the practical face of adaptation. This includes stronger moorings, corrosion-resistant materials, raised electrical systems in flood-prone areas, wave-resistant floating platforms, backup oxygen systems, and emergency power redundancy. For land-based sites, adaptation may require flood barriers, stormwater diversion, backup water storage, or cooling systems for heat-sensitive hatcheries. The objective is not simply to survive one extreme event but to reduce repeated operational disruption over time. A resilient mooring system, for example, should be designed according to updated storm return periods and inspected after severe weather. Temperature-sensitive hatchery rooms may require insulation upgrades, heat recovery ventilation, or automated alarms. Managers should understand that adaptation measures must be matched to local climate projections and site conditions, not copied blindly from another farm. A Mediterranean offshore cage site faces different climate pressures from a freshwater inland hatchery. Good adaptation planning therefore combines engineering judgment, site history, environmental data, and practical contingency planning.

Temperature monitoring systems are among the most important adaptation tools because temperature affects fish metabolism, feed intake, oxygen demand, growth, disease risk, and mortality. In both marine and land-based systems, continuous monitoring allows managers to react early rather than after losses occur. Effective systems can include submerged temperature loggers, multiparameter sondes, remote telemetry, alarm dashboards, and integrated farm management software. In more advanced setups, temperature is monitored alongside dissolved oxygen, salinity, pH, turbidity, and ammonia so that managers can understand combined stress conditions. For example, a summer heat event may coincide with lower oxygen solubility and increased feeding pressure, creating a high-risk period. If the farm has threshold-based alerts, staff can reduce feed, increase oxygenation, adjust stocking strategy, postpone handling, or activate cooling or shading where available. Adaptation becomes much more effective when it is data-led. Records of daily temperatures, extreme events, fish responses, and intervention outcomes also create evidence that the farm is actively managing climate risk—an important point for both Taxonomy alignment and insurance or lender confidence.

DNSH criteria are crucial in adaptation because adaptation investments must not create new significant harm to other environmental objectives. For example, building a hard coastal barrier might protect infrastructure but damage marine habitats or worsen erosion elsewhere. Installing emergency cooling systems might protect fish but create high energy demand if they are inefficient and fossil-fuel dependent. Adding backup generators may improve resilience but increase emissions unless integrated into a broader transition plan. Managers therefore need to assess adaptation measures through a whole-system lens. The best adaptation plans are those that improve resilience while also supporting mitigation, water stewardship, pollution prevention, and biodiversity. Examples include nature-sensitive siting decisions, low-energy monitoring systems, climate-resilient moorings that reduce seabed disturbance, or water management systems that buffer drought without increasing pollution loads. In practical terms, a good adaptation plan should include a written risk register, defined climate hazards, vulnerable assets, response thresholds, designated responsibilities, maintenance plans, training drills, and evidence of review after incidents. At EQF Level 5, learners should be able not only to recognize climate threats but to supervise adaptation planning as a normal part of farm management rather than as a one-off emergency exercise.

### 1.6 Water and Marine Resources — Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS), Water Treatment Cycles, Discharge Monitoring

The sustainable use and protection of water and marine resources is one of the most central EU Taxonomy objectives for aquaculture because water is the medium in which the entire business operates. Every decision on stocking density, feed, cleaning, oxygenation, filtration, medicine use, and waste handling affects water quality directly or indirectly. In practical terms, this objective asks whether the farm uses water efficiently, protects receiving waters, limits harmful discharge, and manages marine resources responsibly. In Zone B, the hatchery is used as the key learning space because hatcheries and juvenile production systems are often intensive and highly dependent on stable water conditions. A modern response to these pressures is the Recirculating Aquaculture System, or RAS. In a well-designed RAS, more than 95% of water may be treated and reused, dramatically reducing freshwater abstraction and limiting discharge to the environment. This is especially valuable in areas facing water scarcity, high intake costs, or strict discharge controls. For farm managers, the key lesson is that water stewardship is not only about having enough water but about controlling water quality throughout the production cycle.

A RAS works through a sequence of treatment stages that remove solids, convert dissolved wastes, stabilize water chemistry, and return water to the culture tanks in a condition suitable for fish health and growth. The exact configuration varies, but common components include drum filters or settlement units for solids removal, biofilters for nitrification, degassing units to remove carbon dioxide, oxygenation systems, UV or ozone disinfection, and pH or alkalinity control. Some systems also include denitrification units and foam fractionation. To a beginner, this can seem complicated, but the basic idea is simple: waste is separated, water is cleaned, and usable water is returned to the fish rather than discharged. The operational challenge is that each part of the cycle must function correctly and be monitored carefully. If solids are not removed quickly, they break down and increase ammonia. If biofilters are overloaded or unstable, nitrite can spike. If disinfection is poor, disease pressure rises. Therefore, Taxonomy alignment in this area depends not just on having a RAS installed but on operating it effectively. Managers need routine control of flow rates, retention times, backwashing schedules, sludge removal, oxygen levels, ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, pH, temperature, and microbial risk.

Discharge monitoring remains essential even in systems with high recirculation because no farm is completely closed. Water may still be discharged during cleaning, filter flushing, emergency events, sludge dewatering, or partial water exchanges. The objective is to prove that discharge is minimized, measured, and controlled. Parameters commonly monitored include total suspended solids (TSS), total nitrogen, ammonium, nitrate, total phosphorus, biological oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen demand (COD), salinity, pH, and where relevant, medicine residues or disinfectant traces. In marine sites, discharge impact may also be linked to local current patterns, dilution rates, and proximity to sensitive habitats. A well-managed farm should have a sampling plan that defines what is tested, how often, by whom, using which method, and where records are stored. For example, a hatchery might sample influent and effluent weekly, verify sensors monthly, and send laboratory confirmation samples quarterly. If a farm can show that 95–98% of water is recirculated and nutrient discharge per tonne of fish has fallen sharply compared with a flow-through system, this becomes strong evidence of substantial contribution under the water objective.

From a management perspective, water and marine resource protection must be treated as a live operational system rather than a compliance afterthought. Staff need to understand why blocked filters, poor sludge removal, leaking valves, overfeeding, or neglected probes can undermine both fish performance and environmental claims. Good practice includes preventive maintenance, probe calibration, alarm testing, backup pumps, emergency bypass procedures, and trend analysis of key water quality indicators. It also includes understanding the marine context beyond the farm gate. For offshore or coastal sites, protecting marine resources means considering currents, benthic deposition, oxygen depletion risk, and interactions with local ecosystems. For land-based hatcheries, it means protecting the river, estuary, or coastal zone receiving treated water. Under the EU Taxonomy, the strongest farms are those that can connect technology, routine monitoring, and environmental outcomes in a clear chain of evidence. If the farm says it protects water, it should be able to show intake reduction, recirculation percentage, discharge data, treatment performance, corrective actions when limits are exceeded, and continuous improvement measures. That is the difference between a sustainability claim and a defensible sustainability practice.

### 1.7 Circular Economy Transition — Sludge-to-Biogas, Fertilizer Byproducts, Packaging Waste Reduction

The transition to a circular economy is about keeping resources in use for as long as possible, extracting maximum value from them, and minimizing the creation of waste. In aquaculture, this objective is highly practical because farms generate several material streams that are often treated as disposal problems: sludge, mortalities, used packaging, damaged nets, feed bags, plastics, ropes, pallets, and sometimes waste heat. A circular approach asks a different question: instead of “How do we get rid of this?”, it asks “How can this stream be reduced, reused, repaired, recycled, or converted into value?” In Zone C, the core learning example is organic sludge capture and processing. Sludge from filters, settlement units, and cleaning operations contains organic matter and nutrients. If discharged or poorly handled, it contributes to pollution. If captured and processed, it can become feedstock for anaerobic digestion, producing biogas and digestate that can be further processed into agricultural fertilizer. This is a textbook circular economy example because a waste stream from aquaculture becomes a resource for energy and nutrient recovery. For professionals, the key message is that circularity is not a theory of recycling bins; it is a redesign of material flows across the farm.

Sludge-to-biogas systems are particularly valuable because they address both waste and energy. In a basic model, collected sludge is thickened and sent to an anaerobic digester where microorganisms break down organic matter in the absence of oxygen. The process produces biogas, mainly methane and carbon dioxide, which can be used for heat, electricity, or upgraded for other energy applications. The remaining digestate can be separated and treated for use as a fertilizer product if it meets quality and safety requirements. The practical metrics here include kilograms or tonnes of sludge captured per month, percentage of total sludge recovered, cubic metres of biogas generated, kilowatt-hours of energy produced, and nutrient content of the recovered fertilizer product. For a farm manager, this means turning a cost centre into a partially productive asset. Even if the farm does not operate its own digester, sending dewatered sludge to a regional biogas plant can still support circularity if traceability is maintained. The important Taxonomy point is that the farm should demonstrate prevention of wasteful disposal and evidence of beneficial material recovery.

Fertilizer byproducts are another important circular pathway because aquaculture sludge contains nitrogen, phosphorus, and organic matter that are valuable in agriculture if processed correctly. Direct use is not always possible due to moisture, pathogen risk, or transport costs, but drying, composting, pelletizing, or digestate treatment can produce a more stable output. This is especially relevant in regions where agriculture requires nutrient inputs and where phosphorus recovery has strategic value. However, managers must understand that circular economy alignment does not mean simply moving waste from one place to another. Quality control, contamination prevention, and proper documentation are essential. If the sludge contains problematic chemical residues or is mixed with unsuitable waste, the recovery pathway may fail. Therefore, separation at source, process control, and partner verification are critical. In practice, a circular farm should know where each waste stream goes, under what conditions it is processed, and what percentage is actually recovered. A material flow map is a useful tool: it shows inputs such as feed, packaging, energy, and water, and outputs such as fish, sludge, plastics, wastewater, mortalities, and recoverable materials.

Packaging waste reduction is often less technically complex than sludge processing but still highly relevant to circular economy performance. Aquaculture farms use large volumes of feed bags, plastic liners, straps, containers, and transport materials. Circular improvement in this area may include switching to reusable bins, bulk feed delivery systems, returnable containers, supplier take-back schemes, segregated plastics collection, and procurement criteria that favour recyclable or recycled-content packaging. Repair and reuse of equipment such as crates, pipes, floats, and nets should also be considered before replacement. Circularity in packaging is especially suitable for immediate action because it often requires more organization than capital. A manager can introduce waste segregation stations, train staff, record monthly packaging waste volumes, and set reduction targets within one production cycle. The broader lesson is that circular economy transition is not one piece of equipment; it is a management mindset that redesigns purchasing, process control, waste handling, and supplier relationships. Under the EU Taxonomy, a farm shows strength in this objective when it can demonstrate that material loops are being closed, disposal is reduced, and waste streams are converted into useful outputs wherever technically and economically feasible.

### 1.8 Pollution Prevention & Control — Zero-Runoff Systems, Chemical Inventory Management, AMR Drug Use Reduction

Pollution prevention and control in aquaculture means stopping harmful substances or excess nutrients from entering water, soil, or the wider ecosystem. This objective overlaps with water protection and circular economy, but it has a more specific focus on contamination risks and emission control. In daily farm reality, pollution can come from nutrient-rich effluent, sludge leakage, chemical spills, disinfectants, fuels, antifoulants, medicines, cleaning agents, and poorly managed mortalities. What makes this objective especially important is that pollution incidents can quickly damage both environmental quality and business credibility. A single spill, overdose, or runoff event can trigger fish stress, local ecological impacts, regulator intervention, and reputational harm. The Taxonomy approach requires farms to move beyond reactive cleanup and implement systems that prevent pollution from occurring in the first place. In Zone C, one of the strongest examples is a zero-runoff system. This means farm design and operation aim to ensure that nutrient-rich water, sludge, and contaminated washdown flows are captured and treated rather than allowed to escape into surrounding land or water bodies.

A zero-runoff system can take different forms depending on the site. In land-based operations, it may involve sealed drainage channels, collection pits, settlement tanks, controlled washdown areas, impermeable flooring, bunded storage zones, and dedicated treatment units for process water. In cage farming support areas, it may involve controlled cleaning stations, waste capture systems, and strict handling protocols during feed, fuel, and net maintenance activities. The principle is that nothing potentially polluting should leave the farm uncontrolled. To prove this in practice, managers need drainage maps, inspection routines, maintenance records, and incident logs. They should know exactly where wash water goes, where sludge is stored, where chemicals are handled, and what barriers exist to prevent accidental release. Key indicators include discharge volume, nutrient concentration, spill frequency, response time, and percentage of wastewater routed through treatment. A farm that claims zero runoff should be able to demonstrate both infrastructure and behaviour: sealed systems alone are not enough if staff bypass procedures, leave valves open, or overtop storage during cleaning.

Chemical inventory management is another central part of pollution prevention. Farms commonly store fuels, lubricants, disinfectants, pH correction products, veterinary medicines, cleaning chemicals, and sometimes laboratory reagents. Without a proper inventory system, chemicals can become an invisible risk: expired products remain on shelves, incompatible substances are stored together, containers leak, and emergency response becomes slow or ineffective. Good management requires a complete chemical register listing product names, quantities, locations, hazard classifications, expiry dates, safety data sheet availability, and designated users. Storage should follow segregation principles, with bunding for liquids, locked medicine cabinets where required, spill kits nearby, and clear labelling. Monthly inspections should verify stock condition and usage. This is not just administrative good practice. It directly affects environmental performance. A leaking drum or incorrect cleaning dose can result in water contamination, fish stress, or worker exposure. The best farms integrate inventory control with procurement so that chemicals are bought in quantities that match actual need and lower-risk alternatives are considered when possible. This supports both pollution prevention and cost control.

Reduction of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) and responsible drug use are now critical sustainability issues in aquaculture. While medicines may be necessary for animal welfare and disease control, overuse or poor use can contribute to resistance development, environmental residues, and market concerns. Pollution prevention in this area means reducing reliance on treatments through better biosecurity, vaccination, stocking density management, water quality control, and rapid disease detection. When medicines are used, they should be prescribed appropriately, recorded accurately, and reviewed after treatment. Metrics such as treatment frequency per production cycle, biomass treated, withdrawal compliance, mortality trends, and vaccine coverage help managers understand whether the farm is progressing toward reduced drug dependence. Farms should also monitor feed management and stress reduction because poor husbandry often leads indirectly to higher disease pressure and medicine use. Under the EU Taxonomy, pollution prevention is strongest when the farm can show systematic control: no uncontrolled runoff, strong chemical governance, evidence-based medicine use, and continuous reduction of avoidable contamination risks. For supervisors, this means building a culture where prevention is routine, records are trusted, and environmental protection is integrated into every operational task rather than left to specialists after a problem occurs.

### 1.9 Biodiversity & Ecosystem Protection — Fallowing Schedules, Benthic Sensor Deployment, *Posidonia oceanica* Restoration Metrics

Protection and restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems is the EU Taxonomy objective that asks aquaculture to look beyond the farm boundary and take responsibility for the condition of the surrounding natural environment. This is especially important in marine and coastal aquaculture, where farm operations can influence seabed conditions, wild species interactions, habitat quality, and ecological recovery. Biodiversity protection is not only about avoiding visible damage. It also involves understanding cumulative effects over time, such as organic enrichment under cages, shifts in benthic communities, pressure on sensitive habitats, or interactions with protected species. In Zone D, the coastal seabed is used as the learning environment because it makes ecosystem impact tangible. Fish farmers may not always see what happens below the cages, but seabed condition is one of the clearest indicators of whether production intensity is compatible with ecological health. This objective therefore demands a longer view: not just “Are fish growing well?” but “Is the ecosystem around the farm remaining functional, diverse, and capable of recovery?”

Fallowing schedules are one of the most practical and proven tools for protecting seabed biodiversity. Fallowing means temporarily leaving a production area empty between cycles so that organic loading pressure is reduced and the seabed has time to recover. In simple terms, if a farm continuously deposits organic matter without a recovery period, oxygen can decline in sediments, benthic communities can shift, and ecosystem quality can deteriorate. By rotating sites or production areas and observing structured fallow periods, the farm creates conditions for natural regeneration. Effective fallowing requires planning at management level: production scheduling, cage rotation, biomass distribution, and post-harvest cleaning all need to be aligned. A fallowing plan should specify minimum durations, trigger conditions, seabed monitoring before restocking, and responsibilities for sign-off. It is not enough to say an area was “left alone for a while.” The plan must be documented and linked to environmental indicators. Farms should compare benthic condition before production, at peak loading, after harvest, and during recovery to determine whether fallowing is adequate. This transforms fallowing from tradition into evidence-based ecosystem management.

Benthic sensor deployment strengthens biodiversity protection because it provides continuous or repeated data on the conditions beneath and around the farm. Depending on system sophistication, this may include dissolved oxygen sensors near the seabed, redox potential monitoring, sediment traps, current meters, camera systems, acoustic mapping, or remote platforms that combine several indicators. These tools help managers detect early signs of overloading rather than waiting for visible ecological decline or inspection findings. For example, if organic deposition increases and oxygen trends downward beneath a cage cluster, stocking distribution or feeding strategy may need adjustment before major damage occurs. Sensors do not replace biological surveys, but they improve timing, responsiveness, and confidence in decision-making. At supervisory level, managers should know where sensors are placed, how often they are cleaned and calibrated, what thresholds trigger action, and how data is interpreted in relation to farm operations. A benthic monitoring programme becomes much more useful when linked to production records, feed input, current conditions, and fallowing decisions. This creates a direct line between farm management and ecosystem response.

*Posidonia oceanica* restoration metrics are especially relevant in Mediterranean contexts because this seagrass species is a protected and ecologically valuable habitat. Posidonia meadows support biodiversity, stabilize sediments, store carbon, and indicate good environmental condition. They are also sensitive to shading, turbidity, nutrient enrichment, physical disturbance, and poor water quality. Where aquaculture operates near such habitats, protecting and restoring them is a strong indicator of serious ecosystem stewardship. Restoration metrics may include mapped area of meadow coverage, shoot density, percentage survival of transplanted patches, leaf length, epiphyte load, sediment stability, and distance from farm impact zones. A farm involved in restoration should not treat this as a public relations exercise. It should work with marine ecologists, establish baseline habitat maps, define realistic targets, and monitor outcomes over time. Most importantly, restoration must sit alongside pressure reduction; there is little value in planting seagrass if the farm continues practices that degrade the habitat. Under the Taxonomy, biodiversity alignment is strongest when the farm can show avoidance, monitoring, recovery, and where necessary, restoration. For aquaculture professionals, this objective ultimately means understanding that environmental responsibility includes the living systems around the farm. A productive site and a healthy ecosystem are not opposites; in the long term, they depend on each other.

## 2. Practical and Cognitive Skills

After completing this module, the learner should be able to:

- Explain the purpose of the EU Taxonomy in plain operational language to farm staff, owners, auditors, and funding bodies.
- Identify the legal foundation of the Taxonomy, including Regulation (EU) 2020/852, and describe why it matters for sustainable finance.
- Distinguish between an activity being environmentally beneficial in general terms and being taxonomy-aligned in documented terms.
- Describe the six environmental objectives and match each one to real aquaculture processes and farm zones.
- Map farm operations into the CircuTech operational zones:
  - Zone A: Energy & Infrastructure
  - Zone B: Hatchery / Water Management
  - Zone C: Waste Processing
  - Zone D: Coastal Seabed / Ecosystems
  - Zone E: Integrated Taxonomy Action Plan
- Conduct a baseline sustainability review of an aquaculture facility using practical operational data.
- Identify the farm’s main resource flows: energy, water, feed, chemicals, waste, sludge, packaging, and biodiversity interfaces.
- Assess whether a farm activity makes a substantial contribution to one or more EU Taxonomy environmental objectives.
- Apply the DNSH principle by checking whether improvements in one area may cause harm in another.
- Recognize minimum safeguard requirements related to governance, labour, and responsible business conduct.
- Build a farm-level evidence file for taxonomy alignment using permits, logs, lab analyses, maintenance records, training records, and monitoring data.
- Design and use a simple compliance checklist for internal farm audits.
- Interpret energy consumption data in relation to production output.
- Compare diesel-based and solar-wind hybrid energy systems using practical metrics such as litres of fuel displaced, kWh produced, and CO2e reduction.
- Identify suitable renewable energy options for remote or coastal aquaculture facilities.
- Evaluate energy efficiency opportunities such as VFDs, efficient pumps, improved aeration control, insulation, and smart monitoring.
- Calculate or interpret key production indicators linked to mitigation, including feed conversion ratio and energy intensity per kg of fish.
- Recommend feed management improvements that reduce both cost and carbon burden.
- Conduct a basic climate risk and vulnerability assessment for an aquaculture site.
- Identify site-specific climate hazards such as storms, marine heatwaves, drought, flooding, salinity shifts, and harmful algal blooms.
- Recommend adaptation measures such as resilient moorings, raised electrical systems, backup oxygen supply, flood barriers, and emergency procedures.
- Interpret temperature monitoring and multiparameter water quality data for climate adaptation decision-making.
- Set threshold-based action triggers for heat stress, oxygen decline, and extreme weather response.
- Explain how adaptation measures must also satisfy DNSH expectations.
- Describe how a RAS works, including solids removal, biofiltration, degassing, oxygenation, and disinfection stages.
- Assess hatchery water use efficiency and calculate recirculation percentages.
- Monitor and interpret core water quality parameters including ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, pH, dissolved oxygen, temperature, and suspended solids.
- Develop a discharge monitoring plan covering sampling points, frequency, parameters, responsibilities, and record storage.
- Interpret discharge results in relation to nutrient loading and marine protection.
- Identify sludge capture points and evaluate whether sludge is being effectively contained and recovered.
- Assess circular economy opportunities in sludge, packaging, equipment, and consumables.
- Explain the process and business value of sludge-to-biogas conversion.
- Track and report sludge volumes, biogas output, digestate handling, and fertilizer recovery potential.
- Identify options for reducing packaging waste through supplier take-back, reusable containers, segregation, and procurement choices.
- Develop a materials flow map showing major inputs, outputs, losses, and recovery opportunities.
- Design or review a zero-runoff system for a hatchery, processing area, or support facility.
- Trace drainage routes and identify uncontrolled release points or spill risks.
- Create and maintain a chemical inventory register with storage, hazard, expiry, and usage controls.
- Verify correct chemical segregation, bunding, labelling, and spill response readiness.
- Evaluate medicine use practices and identify opportunities to reduce antimicrobial dependence through prevention and husbandry.
- Monitor and report treatment frequency, disease trends, vaccine use, and withdrawal compliance.
- Explain AMR risk in practical farm management terms.
- Plan and supervise fallowing schedules based on production cycles and seabed recovery indicators.
- Interpret benthic monitoring data from sensors, cameras, or surveys.
- Use seabed condition evidence to support stocking, feeding, rotation, or restocking decisions.
- Recognize the ecological importance of *Posidonia oceanica* and other sensitive habitats.
- Contribute to restoration planning by understanding baseline mapping, habitat indicators, and recovery monitoring.
- Prepare a taxonomy-aligned action plan for presentation to management, investors, or funding authorities.
- Link technical investments and farm improvements to suitable EU funding pathways:
  - EMFAF
  - LIFE Programme
  - Horizon Europe
  - EIB Green Loans
- Follow the funding logic: assess compliance, document evidence, submit application, support expert verification, and monitor implementation.
- Supervise staff contributions to sustainability data collection and ensure records are complete, credible, and audit-ready.
- Communicate sustainability performance clearly using evidence, metrics, and operational examples rather than vague claims.

## 3. Management and Supervisory Autonomy Outcomes

### Scenario 1: Preparing a Taxonomy-Aligned Investment Plan for Green Finance
The learner can independently lead the preparation of a farm investment case for a solar-wind hybrid upgrade, hatchery water recirculation improvements, and sludge recovery infrastructure. This includes mapping each investment to the correct Taxonomy objective, collecting baseline and forecast performance data, checking DNSH risks, organizing documentary evidence, and presenting the final action plan to owners, lenders, or grant evaluators. The learner can justify why the package fits funding routes such as EMFAF, LIFE, or EIB green finance and can respond confidently to requests for technical clarification.

### Scenario 2: Managing a Climate Stress Event at Farm Level
The learner can independently supervise farm response during a heatwave, storm warning, or abnormal temperature event. This includes reviewing live environmental data, activating predefined thresholds, adjusting feeding and oxygenation, checking resilient infrastructure, coordinating staff duties, protecting animal welfare, and documenting the event for post-incident review. The learner can also assess whether existing adaptation measures are adequate and propose further improvements without waiting for external direction.

### Scenario 3: Leading an Internal Sustainability Audit Across All Farm Zones
The learner can independently organize and supervise an internal audit covering energy, water, waste, pollution control, and biodiversity evidence. This includes assigning responsibilities, checking record quality, identifying compliance gaps, verifying monitoring systems, and producing a corrective action plan with timelines and responsibilities. The learner can distinguish between missing documentation and actual operational weakness, and can prioritize actions that improve both environmental performance and funding readiness.

### Scenario 4: Addressing a Seabed Impact and Biodiversity Risk Issue
The learner can independently manage a case where benthic monitoring shows deterioration beneath cages or near sensitive habitat such as *Posidonia oceanica*. This includes reviewing data, consulting environmental thresholds, adjusting stocking or feeding practices, implementing or extending fallowing, coordinating further monitoring, and documenting the response for regulators, auditors, or investors. The learner can balance production pressures with ecosystem protection and make justified operational decisions that support long-term taxonomy alignment.
