## 2. Practical and Cognitive Skills

After completing this module, the learner should be able to:

- Explain the purpose of the EU Taxonomy in clear operational language to farm staff, managers, auditors, and funding bodies.
- Distinguish between the six environmental objectives and match each objective to real activities in an aquaculture facility.
- Identify which parts of a farm operation correspond to:
  - climate change mitigation,
  - climate change adaptation,
  - water and marine resource protection,
  - circular economy,
  - pollution prevention and control,
  - biodiversity and ecosystem restoration.
- Describe the difference between:
  - substantial contribution,
  - Do No Significant Harm (DNSH),
  - minimum safeguards,
  - technical screening criteria.
- Conduct a basic Taxonomy mapping exercise across Zones A–E of an aquaculture facility.
- Build a simple compliance matrix linking:
  - farm activity,
  - environmental objective,
  - evidence required,
  - responsible staff member,
  - review date.
- Carry out a baseline review of current farm practices against Taxonomy requirements.
- Recognise gaps between current farm performance and likely alignment expectations.
- Gather and organise evidence needed to support an alignment claim, including:
  - energy bills,
  - generator fuel logs,
  - water abstraction and reuse records,
  - discharge monitoring reports,
  - waste transfer notes,
  - sludge treatment records,
  - benthic survey results,
  - chemical inventory records,
  - medicine use logs,
  - biodiversity monitoring reports.
- Calculate or interpret practical sustainability indicators such as:
  - diesel consumption per month,
  - renewable energy share,
  - kWh per tonne of production,
  - feed conversion ratio,
  - percentage water recirculation,
  - discharge nutrient concentration,
  - sludge recovery rate,
  - waste diversion from disposal,
  - fallowing duration,
  - habitat buffer distances.
- Evaluate whether a renewable energy investment is likely to support climate mitigation.
- Assess whether a proposed infrastructure upgrade addresses climate adaptation risks.
- Interpret climate risk factors affecting aquaculture operations, including storms, temperature rise, oxygen stress, and flood exposure.
- Use sensor and monitoring data to support operational decisions related to temperature, dissolved oxygen, and seabed condition.
- Explain how RAS technology contributes to water efficiency and discharge reduction.
- Assess a hatchery or grow-out water management system for reuse, treatment, and release control.
- Trace nutrient and solids flows through a hatchery or land-based facility.
- Identify opportunities to convert aquaculture sludge or organic residuals into biogas or fertiliser pathways.
- Evaluate current packaging use and propose practical circular economy improvements.
- Distinguish between waste disposal, reuse, recovery, and valorisation.
- Design or improve a waste segregation system for aquaculture operations.
- Review chemical storage and handling arrangements for pollution prevention risks.
- Maintain or supervise a chemical register with traceable records.
- Recognise good practice in reducing antimicrobial dependency through prevention, biosecurity, and husbandry.
- Assess whether current runoff, drainage, and containment arrangements are adequate to prevent pollution incidents.
- Interpret benthic and biodiversity monitoring data at a supervisory level.
- Plan a fallowing schedule linked to environmental monitoring outcomes.
- Identify risks to sensitive habitats such as Posidonia oceanica and propose protective measures.
- Prepare a draft Taxonomy-aligned action plan for presentation to senior management or investors.
- Sequence the funding pathway:
  - assess compliance,
  - document evidence,
  - submit application,
  - undergo verification,
  - secure funding.
- Link Taxonomy-aligned investments to relevant EU funding sources such as EMFAF, LIFE, Horizon Europe, and EIB green loans.
- Translate technical farm improvements into language suitable for funding applications and external verification.
- Communicate sustainability performance without exaggeration or unsupported claims.
- Make justified recommendations for short-, medium-, and long-term sustainability investments on an aquaculture farm.

## 3. Management and Supervisory Autonomy Outcomes

On successful completion of this module, the learner should be able to manage the following real-world situations independently:

### Scenario 1: Preparing a Farm Upgrade Plan for Green Finance
The learner is given responsibility for preparing a sustainability-backed investment case for a marine aquaculture facility seeking external finance. The farm wants to install a solar-wind hybrid power system, improve mooring resilience, and upgrade water monitoring. The learner must independently identify which EU Taxonomy objectives are involved, collect operational evidence, assess likely alignment, identify DNSH issues, and compile a structured action plan suitable for submission to management, investors, or a funding programme such as EMFAF or the EIB.

### Scenario 2: Supervising Hatchery Compliance and Water Performance
The learner oversees a hatchery or nursery unit using high volumes of water and is tasked with proving that a new RAS investment supports sustainable water use. The learner must independently monitor recirculation rates, assess treatment performance, review discharge data, organise evidence files, and explain to external stakeholders how the system contributes to water protection while remaining biologically realistic and operationally safe. They must also identify any weak points that could undermine compliance or funding eligibility.

### Scenario 3: Leading a Waste-to-Value and Pollution Control Improvement Programme
The learner is asked to reduce disposal costs and improve environmental performance in the waste processing area. They must independently assess sludge flows, identify circular economy opportunities such as biogas or fertiliser recovery, tighten chemical and runoff controls, and implement traceable procedures for waste handling and pollution prevention. They should be able to justify investments, assign staff responsibilities, establish monitoring indicators, and produce evidence that the facility is moving from waste management to resource recovery.

### Scenario 4: Responding to Biodiversity Risk at a Coastal Production Site
The learner receives monitoring results showing rising benthic stress near cages and possible pressure on nearby Posidonia oceanica habitat. They must independently interpret the environmental data, revise fallowing schedules, recommend operational adjustments, organise further monitoring, and report the situation in a way that supports both ecological recovery and continued compliance. This includes balancing production pressure with habitat protection, documenting actions taken, and demonstrating that the farm is managing biodiversity risks proactively rather than reactively.
